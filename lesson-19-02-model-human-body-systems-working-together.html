📖 Before You Investigate
A short refresher before you head outside
⏱ 8 min
👁️
Section 1
What Is a Body System?

Good observers don't just look. They use as many sensessensesThe five ways your body collects information: sight, hearing, smell, taste, touch. as they safely can. A leaf might look smooth — but feel waxy. A rock might look gray — but feel cold and heavy. Each sense gives you a different kind of observation.

You'll also use tools today. A ruler turns "kind of small" into "4.2 centimeters." A magnifier turns "the edge looks bumpy" into "twelve tiny teeth along one side." Tools turn vague observations into strong evidence.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Which observation is stronger evidence: 'the leaf is medium-sized' or 'the leaf is 6 centimeters long'? Why?"
📓
Section 2
Why Build a Model?

Scientists often record observations in a table instead of long paragraphs. A table forces you to be specific. You can't write "it was pretty" in a column called color. You have to write the actual color — light green, dark green, brown spots near the tip.

That structure pushes you to slow down. And slowing down is how you notice things you would normally miss.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"If you wrote everything you noticed in one big paragraph, what kinds of details might you forget to include?"
✏️
Section 3
Every Model Has Limits

Don't worry if you can't draw well. Scientific sketches are about accuracy, not beauty. A simple drawing with labels and arrows can carry more information than three sentences of writing.

Real-World Connection: Field biologists who study plants, animals, or rocks still sketch in their notebooks every single day. A photo can miss what matters. A sketch shows what the scientist chose to focus on — and that's evidence too.

🔑 Key Concept

Careful observation is the start of every scientific investigation. The more specific your observations, the stronger your evidence becomes — and the better questions you can ask.

🔬 Your Investigation
One specimen. Ten observations. One testable question.
⏱ 25 min

Today you're the scientist. You'll choose one small thing from outside, look at it carefully, and fill a notebook page with real observations. By the end, you should know your specimen better than anyone else in your class.

📷 Reference · Three good specimens
🖼️

Pick something this size — small enough to hold, but interesting enough to study. A leaf, a pebble, a shell, or a piece of bark all work great.
1

Choose your specimen

Find something small from outside or from a window box — a leaf, a pebble, a shell, a piece of bark, a seed pod. Place it on a flat surface where you can see it clearly. Write what you chose at the top of your notebook page.

2

Sketch what you see

Draw your specimen as accurately as you can. It doesn't have to be pretty. Add at least three labels with arrows pointing to specific features (like "small bumps near the edge" or "darker spot near the tip").

3

Fill your observation table

Copy the table below into your notebook. Fill in every row — no skipping. Aim for one strong observation per row. Use the ruler when you can. Use words, not guesses.

4

Spot a question

Look back through your table. What surprised you? Write one testable question your observations made you want to ask. Example: "Do leaves with toothed edges feel rougher than leaves with smooth edges?"

5

Compare and check

Count your observations. Did you reach ten? If not, go back and look again — there's almost always something you missed the first time. Scientists revise as they observe.

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
What is a "system" in the human body?
AA single organ doing one job
BA team of organs working together to do a big job
CThe body's skin
✅ Correct! Systems are teams. The digestive system includes the mouth, stomach, intestines, liver, and more — all working together to handle food.
❌ Not quite. One organ alone isn't a system. And skin is itself an organ. A system is a TEAM of organs working together.
When you eat lunch, your digestive system breaks down the food. But your muscles need that food's energy. What other system delivers it?
AThe circulatory system (blood) carries nutrients to your muscles
BThe skeletal system carries it through your bones
CThe muscles travel to the stomach themselves
✅ Correct! Blood is the body's delivery service. It picks up nutrients from the digestive system and drops them off at every cell that needs them.
❌ Not quite. Bones don't carry nutrients. Muscles don't move around the body. What flows through your whole body, delivering things?
A simple body-system model uses boxes and arrows. What's ONE thing a simple model usually leaves out?
AWhat each organ does
BThe order organs work in
CThe tiny details — like cells, chemicals, or split-second timing
✅ Correct! Models simplify. They show the big idea but leave out small details. That's OK — they're tools for understanding, not perfect copies.
❌ Not quite. Good models DO show what organs do and the order they work in. What can a simple box-and-arrow diagram NOT show?
