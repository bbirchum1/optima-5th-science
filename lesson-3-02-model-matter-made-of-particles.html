A quick guide to thinking like a model-builder
⏱ 8 min
🧩
Section 1
What a Model Is For

A modelmodelSomething scientists use to show what they can't easily see. is anything that helps scientists think about something they can't see or touch directly. A globe is a model of Earth. A drawing of a heart in a science book is a model. A toy car is a model of a real car.

Today you'll build a model of particlesparticleA tiny piece of matter, far too small to see. using small objects you can actually pick up — beans, pennies, paper clips, anything small and the same. Each object will stand in for one particle.

🤔
Stop and Think
"A globe doesn't have mountains you can feel, clouds you can see move, or cities you can visit. So why is it still a useful model of Earth?"
📐
Section 2
Three Arrangements of Matter

The particles inside a rock are packed tightly. They can barely move at all. That's why a rock holds its shape and feels hard. The particles in water are still close together, but they slide past each other — that's why water flows and changes shape to fit any container.

The particles in air are way more spread out. They bounce around fast, with lots of empty space between them. That's how the perfume particles can drift across the room to your nose — they're far apart and constantly moving.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"Which arrangement would make matter the easiest to squeeze into a smaller space? Why?"
⚠️
Section 3
What Models Can't Do

Every model leaves something out. Beans on a table aren't the same as real particles. Real particles are billions of times smaller. They're constantly moving — beans on your table won't be. And real particles can stack, slide, and bump in three dimensions, not just two.

Real-World Connection: Even professional scientists know their models aren't perfect. The point of a model isn't to be exactly right. It's to make a hard idea easier to think about — and to help you explain what you see.

🔑 Key Concept

A model shows the big idea, even if it leaves things out. A particle model lets us see the difference between matter that's packed tight, matter that flows, and matter that spreads out.

Mind-Blowing Fact
In the air around you right now, particles are moving at roughly 1,000 miles per hour — bouncing off each other billions of times per second. You don't feel it because each one is unimaginably tiny.
Go Deeper — When the same matter changes its arrangement

Here's something cool: the particles in ice, water, and steam are exactly the same kind of particle. The only difference is how they're arranged. In ice, they're packed tight in a pattern. In water, they're loose. In steam, they're way spread out and zooming around.

That means when ice melts, no new matter shows up. The particles just stop holding hands and start sliding around. When water boils away, the particles just spread far apart. Same stuff. Different arrangement.

🔬 Build, Sketch, Explain
Three arrangements with your own particles
⏱ 20 min

You're going to build three particle models on your table — one for a rock, one for water, and one for air. Each "particle" will be one of your small objects (a bean, a penny, a paper clip — whatever you have 12 of).

📷 Reference · A homemade particle model
🖼️
Suggested Image — Generate This
Photorealistic top-down photo of 12 small dried beans (kidney or pinto) arranged on a clean wooden tabletop in a neat 3-by-4 grid, all touching each other. Soft daylight, sharp focus. No hands, no text overlay, no white background. Wood grain visible.
Save as: images/3-02-bean-particle-model.jpg
This is what one arrangement looks like — 12 beans pretending to be 12 particles in a rock. Tomorrow you'll do all three.
1

Gather your "particles"

Pick something you have 12 of — dried beans, pennies, paper clips, LEGO bricks, M&Ms, raisins, dry pasta. They should all look the same. Spread them out on a clear table or counter.

2

Model 1: A rock

Arrange all 12 of your particles to show how they're packed in a rock. They should be touching, in a pattern, not moving. Take 10 seconds to study what it looks like. Sketch it in your notebook. Label the sketch "Rock — tightly packed."

3

Model 2: Water

Now slide the particles slightly apart, so they're still close, but not touching in a neat pattern. Imagine they're moving and bumping into each other. Sketch this arrangement too. Label it "Water — close but flowing."

4

Model 3: Air

Spread the particles way out — across the whole table, with lots of space between them. Imagine they're zipping around at full speed. Sketch this arrangement. Label it "Air — spread far apart." Add little arrows showing how fast they'd be moving.

Find your model's limits

Look at your three sketches. What do they show really well? What do they NOT show? (Hint: real particles are moving constantly. Real particles can stack on top of each other. Your beans can't do that.)

Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In a particle model of a rock, how should the particles be arranged?
ASpread far apart, with lots of empty space
BPacked tightly together, barely moving
CClose together but sliding past each other
✅ Correct! Rock particles are squeezed tight in a pattern. They mostly vibrate in place. That's why a rock holds its shape and feels hard.
❌ Not quite. A rock holds its shape — it doesn't flow or drift. Which arrangement of particles would make matter hold its shape like a rock?
A model leaves things out about the real thing. That means a model is:
Auseless — it doesn't show the truth
Bonly good if scientists agree on every detail
Cuseful for showing big ideas, even if it isn't perfect
✅ Correct! Every model leaves something out. That's okay — a model's job is to make a hard idea easier to think about, not to be perfect.
❌ Not quite. All models simplify reality — even the best ones. That's part of what makes a model useful: it shows the main idea without distracting details.
Why can a smell drift across an entire room?
AParticles in the air are spread out and moving fast, so they bounce all the way across the room.
BSmells aren't really matter — they just appear in your nose.
CSmells travel by jumping through walls.
✅ Correct! Smells are particles. Air particles are far apart and zooming around, so they can travel a long way until they reach your nose.
❌ Not quite. Smells ARE matter — tiny particles. The reason they can travel is what the particles in air are doing. Look back at your air model.
