📖 The Lesson
Three states. One trick for telling them apart.
⏱ 15 min
🧊
Section 1
Solids Hold Their Shape

A solidsolidMatter that keeps its own shape and takes up a fixed amount of space. keeps its own shape. A pencil is a pencil whether it's in your hand, on your desk, or in your backpack. A rock stays the same shape whether it's in a bowl or on the floor. That's because the particles inside are packed tight and barely move.

Solids also have a fixed volumevolumeHow much space something takes up.. A baseball takes up the same amount of space no matter where you put it. You can't squish it smaller or stretch it bigger without breaking it.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Is sand a solid? It can be poured like a liquid. But each little grain keeps its own shape. What makes you say solid or liquid?"
💧
Section 2
Liquids Flow and Fit

A liquidliquidMatter that flows and takes the shape of its container. doesn't have its own shape. Pour a cup of milk into a tall glass and it becomes tall. Pour it into a wide bowl and it spreads out. The milk takes the shape of whatever holds it.

But here's the cool part: a liquid still has a fixed amount. One cup of milk is one cup of milk. It might look different in different containers, but the amount stays the same. Particles in a liquid are still close together — they just slide around each other.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"If you pour a quarter-cup of water into a giant pitcher, why is it still a quarter-cup — even though it looks tiny in there?"
💨
Section 3
Gases Spread to Fill

A gasgasMatter that spreads out to fill any container. doesn't just take the shape of a container — it fills it. Blow up a balloon. The gas inside spreads to every corner of the balloon. Put it in a bigger balloon, and it spreads out farther.

Real-World Connection: Air is a gas. So is the steam coming off boiling water. So is the bubble inside a popping bubble. Every gas does the same thing — it spreads out as far as it can, because its particles are way far apart and zooming around.

🔑 Key Concept

Solids keep their own shape and size. Liquids take the shape of their container but keep their amount. Gases spread out to fill any container they're in.

Three states. Three properties. One easy way to tell them apart.

Mind-Blowing Fact
When water freezes into ice, it actually takes up about 9% more space than it did as a liquid. That's why an ice cube floats — and why a forgotten water bottle in the freezer cracks open.
Go Deeper — Things that are tricky to classify

Some matter is hard to label. Honey flows like a liquid — but very slowly. Toothpaste squeezes out like a thick liquid but holds its shape on the brush. Sand looks like it pours, but each grain is a solid. Even glass, which feels rock-solid, is technically a very, very slow liquid (kind of — scientists still argue about it).

This is part of why scientists test multiple properties when they're trying to classify something. Just because it pours doesn't make it a liquid.

🔬 Three Mystery Samples
Use properties to figure out what each one is.
⏱ 12 min

A scientist is asked to identify three sealed mystery containers without opening them. Each container holds one substance — but the scientist only knows what they observed from the outside. Your job is to figure out which is a solid, which is a liquid, and which is a gas, just from her notes.

Sample A

When I tilt the container, this sample sloshes around. It always settles flat across the bottom. When I shake it, it makes a splashing sound. If I tip the container sideways, it pours toward the new low point.

Sample B

I can't see this sample inside the clear container — it looks empty. But when I squeeze the container, it pushes back. When I open the lid briefly, it whooshes out. The container weighs slightly more closed than when it's left open for a while.

Sample C

This sample is a small object I can clearly see in the container. When I tilt the container, it slides as one piece. It always looks like a rectangle. If I shake the container, it bumps the walls but doesn't change shape.

Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
Which state of matter takes the shape of its container but keeps the same amount?
ASolid
BLiquid
CGas
✅ Correct! A liquid pours into any container and takes its shape, but the amount (volume) doesn't change. One cup is still one cup.
❌ Not quite. A solid keeps its own shape. A gas spreads to fill any container — its volume changes. Which one is left?
You pour milk into a tall glass, then into a wide bowl. Which property of the milk changed?
AThe volume changed — there's more milk now.
BThe mass changed — it weighs more.
CThe shape changed — but the amount stayed the same.
✅ Correct! Liquids take the shape of their container. But pouring milk doesn't add or remove milk, so volume and mass stay the same.
❌ Not quite. Pouring doesn't add or remove milk — so volume and mass don't change. The only thing that's different in the two containers is the…?
Why does air (a gas) spread out to fill every corner of a balloon?
AGas particles are far apart and moving fast, so they spread to take up all the available space.
BGas particles are sticky and try to coat the inside of the balloon.
CAir is special and acts differently from other gases.
✅ Correct! Gas particles are spread apart and zooming around. They keep bouncing until they hit the sides of the container — which is why they fill all the space.
❌ Not quite. Think back to your particle model. Gas particles are far apart and fast. Where do they end up when they bounce around inside a container?
