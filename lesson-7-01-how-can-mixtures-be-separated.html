📖 The Lesson
Three properties. Three separating moves.
⏱ 15 min
🧲
Section 1
Use Magnetism for Iron and Steel

The fastest way to get paper clips out of a mixture is a magnet. Hold the magnet near the pile, and the steel clips jump out and stick to it. Everything else — rice, sand, plastic, salt — stays behind.

That trick only works for iron, steel, and a few other metals. So magnetismmagnetismThe property that lets magnets pull on certain materials. is a great first step when something magnetic is mixed in. If nothing in the pile is magnetic, you skip this step.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If your mixture had pennies and paper clips, would the magnet separate them? Why or why not?"
🪣
Section 2
Use Particle Size with a Sieve

When two materials have very different sized pieces — like rice grains vs. fine sand — you can use a sievesieveA strainer with small holes.. The small particles fall through the holes. The big ones get caught on top.

Kitchen strainers, flour sifters, and colanders are all sieves. So is a coffee filter (for super fine pieces) and screens on doors and windows (which sieve out bugs). The trick is picking a sieve with the right size of holes.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"What would happen if you tried to use a coffee filter to separate rocks from sand? Why?"
💧
Section 3
Use Dissolving for Hidden Cases

What if your mixture has salt and sand mixed together? The grains are about the same size — a sieve won't help. Neither sticks to a magnet. So you use a third property: dissolvingdissolvingWhen a soluble material breaks apart and spreads through a liquid..

Pour the mixture into water. The salt dissolves. The sand sinks to the bottom. Strain off the salty water, and you've got pure sand. Let the salty water sit and evaporate, and the salt comes back.

Real-World Connection: Recycling centers use ALL three of these methods. Magnets pull out steel cans. Sieves and screens sort glass shards by size. Water is used to separate plastics that float from heavier materials that sink.

🔑 Key Concept

To separate a mixture of solids, look at the properties of each material. Magnetism, particle size, and solubility are three of the most useful — each one becomes a tool for pulling one material out at a time.

🤯
Mind-Blowing Fact
Recycling factories can process over 100 tons of mixed trash every hour — separating it into steel, aluminum, plastic, paper, and glass using nothing but magnets, sieves, water, and air blowers. Same properties you're learning, just much bigger.
Go Deeper — What if a mixture has FOUR materials in it?

You just use more steps. Say you have a mix of paper clips, rice, sand, AND salt. First, magnet pulls the clips out (Step 1). Then a sieve separates the rice from the sand-salt mixture (Step 2). Then water dissolves the salt from the sand (Step 3). Then a coffee filter or paper towel separates the sand from the salty water (Step 4).

The order matters. Most scientists pick the easiest step first — usually the magnet — to make later steps simpler.

Three mixtures. Three plans. No picking by hand.

Three scientists in different labs each have a tough mixture to separate. Your job is to plan their separation step-by-step. For each mix, pick the tool(s) they should use and explain which property does the work.

A bowl of dried beans mixed with steel paper clips. Both pieces are about the same size, but the beans are not magnetic.

Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
You have a pile of paper clips mixed with dried lentils. What's the FASTEST way to separate them?
APour them into water — the clips will dissolve.
BUse a magnet — it will pull the clips out and leave the lentils behind.
CUse a sieve — the clips will fall through and the lentils will stay.
✅ Correct! Paper clips are steel — they're magnetic. The lentils aren't. A magnet pulls the clips out in seconds, leaving the lentils.
❌ Not quite. Paper clips don't dissolve in water. Sieves only help if particles are different sizes — these are similar. Which tool uses magnetism?
You have a mixture of pebbles and sand. Why is a SIEVE the right tool?
ASand is magnetic and pebbles aren't.
BSand dissolves in water and pebbles don't.
CSand is small enough to fall through the holes, and pebbles are too big.
✅ Correct! That's exactly how a sieve works — small particles fall through, big ones get caught on top. The property it uses is particle size.
❌ Not quite. Sand isn't magnetic. Sand doesn't dissolve in water. What sieves actually do is sort things by SIZE.
Salt and sand are jumbled together. Both are dry and roughly the same size. Which method works?
AAdd water — the salt dissolves, the sand doesn't. Then strain off the sand.
BUse a magnet — salt is magnetic.
CUse a sieve — salt is bigger than sand.
✅ Correct! Water separates them by solubility. Salt dissolves; sand stays solid. Strain out the sand, and you can recover the salt later by letting the water evaporate.
❌ Not quite. Salt isn't magnetic. Salt and sand grains are similar sizes — a sieve won't help. What property is DIFFERENT between salt and sand?
