Big Question
Think About This…

When you heat or cool a material, the matter changes. Sometimes you can go right back to how it started. Sometimes you cannot. How do you know which kind of change you are looking at?

Keep this question in mind while you work today.

📖 The Lesson
Why the scale never lies about matter
⏱ 15 min
🛡️
Section 1
Heat and Cold Can Change Matter

Temperature changes matter all the time. When you heat something up, its particles move faster and spread apart. When you cool it down, the particles slow down and move closer together. That is why ice melts when it warms and water freezes when it cools.

But heat does something else too. Sometimes heat can break apart the way particles are stuck together and let them join in a new way. When that happens, you get a brand-new kind of matter — not just the old matter in a new shape.

🤔
Stop and Think
"You can melt an ice cube and freeze the water back into ice. Can you un-toast a piece of toast? Why might one work and the other not?"
✂️
Section 2
Two Kinds of Change

A physical changephysical changeA change in shape, size, or form — same kind of matter. changes how something looks but not what it is. Melting ice is a physical change. The ice and the water are both H₂O. The particles just moved farther apart. Most physical changes are reversible — you can undo them and get the starting material back.

A chemical changechemical changeA change that makes a brand-new substance. is different. Heat (or sometimes cold or mixing) can make particles snap apart and join in a new way. Now you have a new kind of matter, not the old kind. Toasting bread, cooking an egg, burning wood, and rusting iron are all chemical changes. They are very hard to undo.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"You melt a chocolate bar in a bowl. You put it in the fridge and it gets hard again. You bake a cake with that same chocolate. Now you cannot get the chocolate bar back. What is different about the second change?"
📦
Section 3
Clues That a New Substance Formed

How do you know when a chemical change has happened? Scientists look for clues. A new color, a new smell, bubbles where there were none before, light or heat being given off, or a material that is suddenly very different. One clue alone might not prove it. But two or three clues at the same time? That is strong evidence.

Look at toast. The bread is soft, pale, and smells like bread. The toast is hard, brown, and smells like toast. New color, new smell, new feel. That is a chemical change. The heat made some of the sugar and starch particles join in new ways. You can't go back.

Real-World Connection: Cooking is full of chemical changes. When you bake cookies, the dough goes in soft and sweet — and comes out crispy, golden brown, and smelling completely different. That is not just heat moving particles around. That is heat making new substances.

🔑 Key Concept

Temperature can change matter in two ways. A physical change makes a new shape but the same kind of matter — usually reversible. A chemical change makes a brand-new substance — usually not reversible. Clues like color, smell, and bubbles help you tell the difference.

🤯
Mind-Blowing Fact
Cooking a single egg involves about five different chemical changes happening at once. The proteins in the white change first (around 145°F), then the yolk, then the sulfur particles release that 'eggy' smell. Every kitchen is a little chemistry lab.
Go Deeper — What about cold? Can cold cause a chemical change?

Heat causes most chemical changes you see, but cold can too. Freezer burn is one example. When food sits in the freezer too long, the water inside slowly leaves the food and a chemical change happens at the surface — making dry, leathery patches. Cold-pack chemical reactions (the kind in instant ice packs) actually use a chemical change to make things cold.

Still, in the kitchen and in most of your daily life, heat is the main thing that drives chemical changes. Cooking, baking, burning, and toasting are all driven by heat. Cooling things down usually stops chemical changes — that's why we put food in the fridge.

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
Which of these is a chemical change?
AMelting butter in a pan
BCooking an egg until it's firm and white
CCrushing a cracker into crumbs
✅ Correct! Cooking an egg changes the proteins permanently. New color, new texture, no going back — those are chemical-change clues.
❌ Not quite. Which one makes a new substance that you cannot turn back? Look for new color, new smell, or new feel.
Which change is reversible?
AMelting chocolate, then putting it in the fridge to harden
BBurning a piece of wood in a campfire
CBaking flour, eggs, and sugar into a cake
✅ Correct! Melting and re-freezing chocolate is a physical change. Same chocolate particles, just moving differently when warm or cool. You can repeat it as many times as you like.
❌ Not quite. Reversible means you can undo it. Can you un-burn a log? Can you un-bake a cake? Which option could you go back and forth on?
Which is the strongest clue that a chemical change happened?
AThe material got bigger or smaller
BThe shape changed
CA new color and a new smell appeared
✅ Correct! A brand-new color and a brand-new smell together are very strong evidence that the particles rearranged into new substances. Shape and size can change without a chemical change.
❌ Not quite. Lots of physical changes can change the shape or size. What you want is a clue that suggests a brand-new substance is there.
