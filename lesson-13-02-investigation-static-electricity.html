📖 Before You Begin
Safety check and the three-test plan
⏱ 7 min
⚖️
Section 1
Safety First

Today's three tests use a balloon (or two), a plastic comb, small bits of torn paper, and a thin stream of water from a faucet. They're safe but a few rules apply. Don't rub a balloon on someone else's face. Don't stick a charged balloon to electronics or a TV screen. Don't let kids near the faucet alone — adult supervision for the water test.

If your hair is very short or you're bald, you can use a wool sweater or fleece blanket instead. They work just as well for building charge on a balloon. If you don't have a balloon, a plastic comb run through hair is the next best thing.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Why might a balloon work better for static tests than a glass cup or a metal spoon?"
📦
Section 2
The Three Tests at a Glance

You'll run three quick tests. Test A — Pull Up Paper: rub a balloon on hair and hold it just above small paper bits. They jump up to the balloon! Test B — Push Apart Balloons: rub two balloons on hair, hang them next to each other on string — they swing apart. Test C — Bend Water: rub a plastic comb on hair, then hold it near a thin stream of water — the water curves toward the comb.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"Which test do you think will be the most surprising? Which might be the trickiest to set up?"
📊
Section 3
Recording What You See

For each test, you'll fill out a row of a table. Three columns: the test, what you saw happen, and whether it was an attraction (pull) or a repulsion (push). Sketch each test in your notebook — a quick drawing is great evidence.

Real-World Connection: The same kind of test you're doing today is used in factories that make computer chips. Engineers have to control static very carefully — even a tiny spark could damage a chip worth millions. They use the same balloon-on-hair trick to test if their static-control mats are working.

🔑 Key Concept

To prove static was at work, look for movement without touching. Paper bits jumping up to a balloon. Balloons swinging apart on their own. Water bending sideways. All evidence of invisible electric force.

Mind-Blowing Fact
The static charge a balloon picks up from your hair is about 20,000 volts — much higher than a wall outlet. So why doesn't it shock you? Because it has almost no current (it's not flowing). High voltage with low current is harmless. That's why static zaps are surprising but never really dangerous.
Go Deeper — What if it's humid?

If your house is humid (lots of moisture in the air) or if it's a rainy day, static tests don't work as well. The water in the air conducts charge away from your balloon as soon as it builds up — so the balloon never gets very charged.

To get the best results, try the tests on a dry day. Or run a hair dryer (no heat, just air) over your hair first to dry it. Cooler, drier air = better static.

🔬 Three Energy Tests
Three tests. Three different results. One big idea.
⏱ 20 min total

Gather your materials. You'll need: 2 inflated balloons (any color), string or yarn (~12 inches per balloon), a plastic comb, scissors and a piece of paper (to tear into small bits), and access to a kitchen sink with a faucet. Plus your hair (or a wool sweater).

⚠️

Before you start: Make sure your testing area is clear — no pets, no breakables, no faces in the way of Test A. Don't shine the flashlight into anyone's eyes. Tap the glass gently — a hard tap can chip or crack it.

Your three test setups ready to go. Each one only takes a minute.
1

Test A — Pull Up Paper Bits

Tear a piece of paper into very small bits (smaller than a fingernail). Spread them on a table or plate. Rub a balloon on your hair for 8–10 seconds. Hold the balloon a few inches above the paper bits — but DO NOT touch them. Watch! Write down what happens.

2

Test B — Flashlight Heat

Place a piece of dark paper on a table. Turn on a flashlight and hold it 2-3 inches above the paper, beam pointing down. Wait 60 seconds. Carefully touch the spot where the beam was hitting. Now touch a different spot the beam was NOT on. Write down what you felt.

3

Test C — Bend a Water Stream

Place a clean glass on the table. Hold a metal spoon by the handle. Gently tap the side of the glass. Listen. Write down what you heard. Try tapping a different part of the glass — does the sound change?

4

Compare your results

Look back at all three tests. Each one started with energy in some form. Each one ended with motion, warming, or sound. Write down which form of energy started each test and what kind of change came out.

Find the common pattern

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In Test A, the paper bits jumped up to the balloon. Why?
AThe paper bits were secretly charged the whole time
BThe balloon had extra negative charge, which attracted the paper bits
CThe balloon physically blew the bits with a tiny gust of air
✅ Correct! Rubbing the balloon on hair gave it extra negative charge. The charged balloon pulls on opposite charges in the paper bits — so the bits leap up to the balloon. No touching needed.
❌ Not quite. Paper bits aren't charged on their own. And the balloon doesn't blow air. Where did the extra charge come from?
In Test B, both balloons swung APART. What does that tell you about their charges?
AThe balloons had the same kind of charge — like charges repel
BThe balloons were full of air, which pushed them apart
CThe balloons had opposite charges
✅ Correct! Both balloons were rubbed on the same hair, so both got the same kind of (negative) charge. Like charges always repel. The balloons push each other away.
❌ Not quite. The air in the balloons can't push them apart from a distance. And opposite charges would ATTRACT, not push apart.
In Test C, the water bent toward the comb. Water doesn't have a fixed charge. How did this work?
AThe charged comb pulls on the positive ends of the water molecules, bending the stream sideways
BThe comb makes wind that pushes the water
CWater is magnetic
✅ Correct! Water molecules have slightly positive and slightly negative ends (they're "polar"). The charged comb pulls the positive ends sideways — bending the whole stream.
❌ Not quite. The comb doesn't blow wind. And water isn't magnetic. But water molecules DO have positive and negative parts inside them.

Bonus — make your own electroscope.
🌟
Bonus video coming soon
📹 Suggested content (30–60 sec) Quick congratulations + an extension. Build a simple "electroscope" out of: a glass jar, a paperclip bent into a hook, two thin strips of foil hanging from the hook. Touch the hook with a charged balloon. The foil strips spread apart! Why? Both strips picked up the same charge, and like charges repel. Encourage students to try this at home with parent help. It's the world's first static-detector — invented in the 1700s.
