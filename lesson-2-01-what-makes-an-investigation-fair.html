📖 The Lesson
Variables, controls, and trials — how scientists keep things fair
⏱ 15 min
📏
Section 1
One Thing Changes. Everything Else Stays.

A fair testfair testA test where only one thing changes, while everything else is kept the same. follows a simple rule: only one thing changes. That thing is called the variablevariableSomething that could change in an investigation.. Everything else stays the same on purpose — those are your controlscontrolSomething kept the same in every trial so it doesn't affect the results..

Say you're testing which kind of soil grows beans best. The soil type changes — that's your variable. But the amount of water, the size of the pot, the kind of seed, the spot on the windowsill — all of those should be exactly the same. Otherwise you won't know what made the difference.

🤔
Stop and Think
"In Mia and Theo's airplane test, what was changing? List every single thing you can think of."
🔁
Section 2
Why You Do It More Than Once

Even when a test is fair, doing it just once isn't enough. Lots of little things you can't control might still wiggle the results — a tiny breeze, a slight slip of your hand, a marble that wobbled a little. That's why scientists use repeated trialsrepeated trialsDoing the same test more than once to see if the result is reliable..

If you drop the same paper airplane three times and it flies about the same distance each time, your evidence is strong. If it flies 3 meters, then 10 meters, then 4 meters, something is off — and one test would have given you a wrong answer.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"If a test gives you three very different numbers, what should you do — pick the one you like best, average them, or run the test again? Why?"
⚗️
Section 3
Investigation Isn't Just Experiments

Not every scientific investigation is a controlled experiment. Some scientists can't change anything — they just watch and record. A scientist studying volcanoes can't make a volcano erupt. A bird-watcher can't speed up the seasons. They use careful observation instead, taking notes over a long time and looking for patterns.

Real-World Connection: Scientists studying the Florida Everglades have been collecting the same kinds of measurements — water levels, bird counts, plant growth — for decades. Each year's careful notes become evidence that helps explain how the ecosystem changes.

🔑 Key Concept

A fair test changes one variable and keeps everything else the same. Repeated trials make your evidence stronger. Together, these are what turn a guess into a real conclusion.

Mind-Blowing Fact
Some medical studies use over 30,000 repeated trials — one for each person in the study — before scientists feel confident that a new medicine actually works.
Go Deeper — Why not just one test?

You might think: "If I drop a ball and it bounces 30 cm, that's the answer — done." But the next time, it might bounce 28 cm, or 32 cm. Tiny things you can't control matter more than you'd guess: how flat the ground is, how exactly you let go, whether the ball was spinning a tiny bit.

That's why even short investigations use 3 or 5 trials. The first answer is just a starting point. The pattern across all the trials is the real evidence.

🔬 Find What's Unfair
Two real-kid tests. One has a problem. (Maybe both do.)
⏱ 12 min

Two students in another class tried to answer the same question: "Which kind of paper towel soaks up the most water?" They each did it differently. Read both tests carefully, then look for what's fair — and what's not.

Jordan's Test

Jordan took one square of Brand A paper towel and dropped 10 drops of water on it. Then he took one square of Brand B and put it under the sink with the faucet running for 3 seconds. He squeezed the water out of each one and decided Brand B held more water. He did each test one time.

Riley's Test

Riley took one square of Brand A and one square of Brand B. He put each one flat on a plate. He used an eyedropper to add water, one drop at a time, until each paper towel could not hold any more. He counted the drops. He repeated the whole test three times for each brand.

Spot the differences

Copy the comparison table into your notebook. Fill in each row for both students. Did they each use the same amount of water? The same way of measuring? Did they repeat?

Decide whose test is fair

Look at your table. Which student's test was actually fair? Which student would have stronger evidence to back up a conclusion? Be ready to defend your answer.

3

Fix the unfair test

Pick the test you think is unfair. Write 2–3 specific changes that would make it fair. (For example: "use the same amount of water for each.") Don't change the question — only the procedure.

4

Make a claim

Write one sentence answering: "Whose evidence about paper towels can we actually trust?" Use the words "variable," "control," or "repeated trials." You'll use this in your CER.

Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
A fair test means that only one thing is allowed to do what?
Abe measured
Bchange between trials
Cstay the same in every trial
✅ Correct! In a fair test, only one variable changes. Everything else has to be kept the same — those are the controls.
❌ Not quite. Think about Mia and Theo's airplane test — what was the problem? Too many things were different at once.
You test whether warm water dissolves sugar faster than cold water. Which of these MUST be kept the same?
Athe temperature of the water
Bwhich water dissolves sugar faster
Cthe amount of sugar you add to each cup
✅ Correct! The sugar amount is a control — it has to be the same in each cup. Temperature is the variable (the thing you're testing).
❌ Not quite. The water temperature is the variable you're testing — it's supposed to change. Look for the thing that should be exactly equal in both cups.
A scientist runs a test once. The result is 40 cm. Why might she still want to repeat the test?
AOne trial might be off because of small things she can't control. Repeating shows if 40 cm is reliable.
BShe wants to use up all her materials.
COne trial is always enough for any scientific test.
✅ Correct! Repeated trials check whether the first result holds up. If the next two trials also land near 40 cm, the evidence is much stronger.
❌ Not quite. Repeated trials aren't about materials or being "enough" — they're about checking if the first answer was reliable.
