📖 Before You Measure
A quick scale primer and the three-trial plan
⏱ 7 min
⚖️
Section 1
How to Use a Kitchen Scale

Turn the scale on. If it has a button labeled "g," "unit," or "mode," press it until you see grams (g). Place your container on the scale. Then press tare or zero — the display will reset to 0 with the container on top. Now any item you add reads only the item's massmassHow much stuff is in something., not the container's.

If your scale doesn't have a tare button, just write down what it says with only the container, then subtract that number from your later reading. Either way works.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Why would you weigh the scaled-down value (with tare) instead of just the total? What does it make easier?"
📦
Section 2
Why Containers Stay Closed

For two of your three trials (the ice and the salt water), you'll put everything in a closed systemclosed systemA sealed container — nothing can escape. — a sealed zip-top bag or a cup with a tight lid. If the container is open, water vapor can escape during the change. Then the scale would show LESS mass — not because matter disappeared, but because some left the container.

This is why scientists testing conservation almost always use sealed containers. It removes the doubt.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"If you melted the ice cube on an open plate instead of in a sealed bag, would the scale show 95 g after? Why or why not?"
📊
Section 3
Recording & Graphing Your Data

You'll write all six measurements (3 trials × 2 readings each) in a data table. Then you'll draw a simple bar graph — two bars per trial, one for "before" and one for "after." If matter is conserved, the bars should be the same height for each trial pair.

Real-World Connection: Pharmacists weigh medicines, food scientists weigh recipe ingredients, and pollution scientists weigh air samples — all the same way you'll do today. Careful before-and-after measurement is the most reliable evidence in science.

🔑 Key Concept

To test conservation of matter, measure mass before, cause a change in a sealed container, and measure mass again. Same number = conservation confirmed.

Mind-Blowing Fact
Modern lab scales can detect a mass change of 0.0001 grams — that's about the weight of a single eyelash. With tools that precise, scientists can spot even tiny conservation violations (which never happen).
Go Deeper — What if you don't have a scale?

You can still investigate conservation with a homemade balance. Tie a string to the center of a stiff ruler. Hang two identical cups from the ends (use string or hooks). When both cups have equal mass, the ruler hangs level.

Put your sealed bag (with ice in it) in one cup, and add stuff to the other cup until the ruler is level. That's how much your sealed bag weighs — without numbers. Then wait for the ice to melt and check if the ruler is still level. If conservation holds, it should be.

🔬 The Three-Trial Mass Lab
Six measurements. One big conclusion.
⏱ 35 min total (with 30 min ice wait)

Get your scale, ice cube, sealed bag, lidded cup, salt, water, and a piece of paper ready. Trial 2 takes the longest because you have to wait for the ice to melt — start that first.

⚠️

Before you start: Use cool tap water, not hot. Make sure your container lids are screwed on tight or zip seals are fully closed. If you spill water on the scale, dry it right away — scales don't love getting wet. Tare (zero) the scale before each measurement so your container's mass isn't counted.

Your lineup before Trial 1. Scale ready, ice in a sealed bag, salt in a lidded cup, paper waiting.
1

Start Trial 2 first (ice takes time)

Put an ice cube into the zip-top bag and seal it tight. Place the bag on the scale. Tare/zero the scale or note the reading. Write that number in your notebook as Trial 2 — Before. Set the bag aside on the counter to melt. You'll come back to it.

2

Trial 1 — Crumple the paper

Lay the flat paper on the scale. Record the mass as Trial 1 — Before. Pick the paper up, crumple it into a tight ball with your hands. Place the crumpled ball back on the scale. Record as Trial 1 — After.

3

Trial 3 — Dissolve the salt

Pour ¼ cup of water into your cup. Add 1 teaspoon of salt. Snap the lid on tight. Put the closed cup on the scale. Record as Trial 3 — Before. Pick the cup up and gently shake or swirl until the salt fully dissolves (about 15–20 seconds). Place it back on the scale. Record as Trial 3 — After.

4

Finish Trial 2 — Weigh the melted bag

By now, the ice cube has been sitting for a while. Check it — it should be mostly or fully melted into water inside the still-sealed bag. Place the bag back on the scale. Record as Trial 2 — After. Don't open the bag!

Trial Before (g) After (g) Difference
📄 Trial 1 — Paper
🧊 Trial 2 — Ice (sealed)
🧂 Trial 3 — Salt water (sealed)
📊

Draw a quick bar graph in your notebook: Three pairs of bars, one pair per trial. Y-axis = mass in grams. X-axis = trial name. The "Before" and "After" bars in each pair should be (almost) exactly the same height. Use two colors — one for "Before," one for "After."

5

Look for the pattern

Compare your before/after numbers in each row. They should be the same — or very close. A 1 g difference is normal scale wobble. A 5 g difference probably means something leaked or the bag wasn't fully sealed. Look at your bar graph: do the pairs look like twins?

6

Make a claim

Write one sentence: "Across all three trials, the mass before equaled the mass after — which means ___." This will become part of your CER.

Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In your lab, why was it important that the bag with the ice cube stay SEALED?
ATo keep the ice from melting too fast
BSo no water vapor could escape — making sure every particle was still in the bag
CSo the scale wouldn't get wet
✅ Correct! In a sealed bag, no matter can escape. That's why the after-mass equals the before-mass — every particle of water is still inside the bag.
❌ Not quite. The sealed bag isn't really about speed or splashing. Think about WHY the mass stays the same — what would happen if vapor could leak out?
Your three trial results: paper 5→5, ice 30→30, salt water 105→105. What do these numbers tell you?
AMatter is conserved across all three changes — the mass before equals the mass after every time.
BThe scale must be broken — three identical readings is impossible.
CConservation only applies to paper, not to ice or salt.
✅ Correct! All three trials show conservation. Different kinds of physical changes, same outcome — the mass doesn't budge.
❌ Not quite. Three matched pairs aren't a broken scale — they're evidence of a real pattern. The pattern is the whole point of conservation.
Your friend ran the SAME ice trial but left the bag open instead of sealing it. Her scale said 30 g before, 27 g after. What happened?
AConservation doesn't apply to ice.
BHer scale was wrong.
C3 g of water evaporated out of the open bag. Matter was still conserved overall — it just left her bag.
✅ Correct! Matter is still conserved — but in an open container, some of it (about 3 g of water vapor) drifted out into the air. Her scale just couldn't catch it.
❌ Not quite. Conservation always applies — but only inside a sealed container. The "missing" 3 g didn't disappear; it left the bag. Where could it have gone?
