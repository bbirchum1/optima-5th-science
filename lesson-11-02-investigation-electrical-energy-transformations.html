📖 Before You Begin
Safety check and the three-device plan
⏱ 7 min
⚖️
Section 1
Safety First

Today's three observations use everyday electrical devices in your home. They are safe, but a few rules apply. Never stick anything into an outlet. Don't unplug or move a device without asking a parent first. Don't touch the inside of a device — keep your hands on the outside only.

For devices that produce heat (hair dryers, toasters, lamps that get hot), be extra careful — don't touch the hot parts. If you don't have one of the suggested devices, swap in something similar. A flashlight works for the light test. A phone or computer speaker works for the sound test. Even a regular fan or hair dryer works for the motion test.

🤔

Section 2
The Three Observations at a Glance

You'll observe three devices. Device A — A Light: a lamp, a flashlight, or even a phone screen turning on. Device B — A Speaker: a TV, a radio, a Bluetooth speaker, or your phone playing music. Device C — A Fan or Motor: a desk fan, a hair dryer (no heat needed), an electric toothbrush, or a blender.

Section 3
Recording Your Observations

For each of your three devices, you'll fill out a row of a table. Three columns: the device, the form of energy you observed (light, heat, sound, or motion), and a clue from your own senses that proves it.

Real-World Connection: When engineers design a new appliance, they test it the same way you will today. They turn it on. They watch carefully. They listen. They feel for heat. They make notes. Then they figure out if the appliance is doing its job well.

🔑 Key Concept

To prove electricity transformed, look at the device when it's on. Does it glow? That's light. Does it warm up? That's heat. Does it make sound? That's sound. Does it move? That's motion. Many devices show more than one form at once.

Mind-Blowing Fact
A single Bluetooth speaker can transform electrical energy into about 20,000 different sound vibrations per second. Your ear picks up every one. Your brain sorts them into voices, drums, and melody — instantly. All from a thin paper cone shaking inside a plastic box.
Go Deeper — Why doesn't a lamp also make a loud sound?

Each electrical device is built to transform energy in one specific way. A lamp has a bulb designed to glow — not to vibrate, not to spin. A speaker has a cone designed to vibrate. A fan has a motor designed to spin. The INSIDE of each device decides what comes out.

If you took apart a lamp and put speaker parts inside instead, you could make the same electrical plug produce sound instead of light. Engineers do this all the time — they pick what they want the device to do, then design it accordingly.

1

Device A — Observe a Light

Pick any light source. Turn it on. Watch the glow. After about a minute, hold your hand a few inches from the bulb area — does it feel warm? Write down what you see and feel. List every form of energy the electricity transformed into.

2

Device B — Observe a Speaker

Place a piece of dark paper on a table. Turn on a flashlight and hold it 2-3 inches above the paper, beam pointing down. Wait 60 seconds. Carefully touch the spot where the beam was hitting. Now touch a different spot the beam was NOT on. Write down what you felt.

3

Device C — Observe a Fan or Motor

Pick a fan, hair dryer (heat OFF), or small motor. Turn it on. Watch the motion. Listen for hum. Feel the moving air (or hold your hand near the motor — does the motor area warm up?). Write down everything you see, hear, and feel.

4

Compare your three devices

Look back at your three observations. All three started with electrical energy. Each one ended with a different form. Which device produced the MOST transformations (light, heat, sound, AND motion)? Which device was the most "single-purpose"?

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In your observation, what form did the lamp's electricity transform into?
ASound energy
BMostly light energy (plus a little heat)
CMechanical energy (motion)
✅ Correct! A lamp's main job is to make light. Some of the electrical energy slips into heat too, which is why bulbs feel warm.
❌ Not quite. Lamps don't play sounds or spin things. What's the OBVIOUS thing a lamp produces?
When you touched a speaker that was playing music, you could feel it vibrating. What does that prove?
ASound is just air moving on its own
BThe speaker is making sound by physically vibrating (a transformation from electricity to motion to sound)
CSpeakers are powered by chemical energy
✅ Correct! The vibrations you feel are how sound is created. The electrical energy makes a part inside the speaker move (vibrate) — those tiny motions push the air, and your ear picks up the result as sound.
❌ Not quite. Speakers don't make sound by magic — they vibrate. Feel the vibration with your hand, that's the evidence.
A hair dryer makes warm, fast-moving air. After running it for a minute, the motor area feels warm too. Which list shows ALL the forms its electricity transforms into?
AHeat + motion + sound
BLight + sound only
CJust motion
✅ Correct! A hair dryer does THREE transformations at once: the heating coil makes heat, the fan motor makes motion, and the whole thing makes sound. The warm air is heat + motion combined.
❌ Not quite. Listen to a hair dryer — it makes noise. Feel near the motor — it gets warm. Feel the air — it moves. THREE jobs, all at once.
