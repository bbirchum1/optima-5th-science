The Three-Step Plan

Setup video coming soon
2
📖 Before You Start the Lab
Three steps in the right order
⏱ 7 min
🧲
Step 1
Magnet First — Always

Start with the magnet. It's the cleanest tool — nothing else gets wet or moved around. Hold the magnet right above the mixed bowl. The paper clips jump up to it and stick. Move them to your "clips" dish.

If you started with water first, the clips would get wet and harder to pick out later. Always pull out the magnetic stuff FIRST when you have it.

🤔
Stop and Think
"What might happen if you tried to use the magnet AFTER adding water? Why?"
💧
Step 2
Water Next — Salt Dissolves

Now you have just rice and salt in the bowl. They're both small. A sieve probably wouldn't separate them well — they're too similar in size. But one of them dissolves in water and the other doesn't.

Pour the rice + salt into your cup of water. Stir gently for a few seconds. The salt dissolvesdissolvingThe salt breaks into tiny pieces and spreads through the water. and disappears. The rice grains stay solid and sink to the bottom or float (rice can do either).

🧪
Stop and Reason
"After stirring, is the salt really gone? How do you know it's still there?"
🪣
Step 3
Filter Last — Catch the Rice

Slowly pour the water + rice mixture through your strainer (or a coffee filter inside a strainer) over an empty bowl. The water and dissolved salt fall through. The rice gets caught on top.

You now have three piles: paper clips (dish 1), rice (in the strainer or its dish), and salty water (the cup below). If you want the salt back as a solid, leave the salty water on a sunny windowsill for a day or two — when the water evaporates, salt crystals appear at the bottom.

Real-World Connection: Sea salt is made this exact way. Salt water is poured into shallow pools called salt flats, the sun evaporates the water, and the salt crystals get scraped up.

🔑 Key Concept

Order matters. Start with the easiest, cleanest tool (the magnet) when something magnetic is mixed in. Then use water-based tools last, because once things are wet, they're harder to separate further.

Here's the full three-step flow for separating rice + clips + salt. Tap each step to find out exactly what to do — and what property is doing the work.

🤯
Mind-Blowing Fact
Industrial salt farms make table salt by spreading sea water across huge shallow pools. After about a month, the water evaporates and workers harvest tons of salt crystals — the same process you'll see on your windowsill, just enormous.
Go Deeper — What if you don't have a strainer?

You can build one. A coffee filter inside a cup or funnel works perfectly. A paper towel folded into a cone shape works too. Even a piece of cheesecloth or a clean kitchen rag stretched across a bowl will catch rice grains.

The trick is making sure the holes are smaller than rice grains but bigger than salt water (which can flow as a liquid through any of those). All these tools work because of the same property: particle size.

🔬 Run Your Three-Step Separation
Start mixed. End with three clean piles.

1

Make the mystery mix

Pour 1 tablespoon of dry rice into one of your bowls. Add 5–6 paper clips. Add 1 teaspoon of salt. Stir gently with the spoon until everything is mixed together. This is your starting mixture.

2

Magnet step — pull the clips

Hold your magnet about an inch above the mixed bowl. Slowly move it across the surface. The paper clips should jump up and stick. Move them to a clean dish. Keep doing this until you can't pick up any more. Count the clips you got — make sure it matches what you started with.

3

Water step — dissolve the salt

Pour your cup of water into the bowl with the remaining rice + salt. Stir gently with a spoon for 10–15 seconds. Watch carefully — the salt grains should disappear. The rice stays whole. (If you taste a drop on your finger, it'll be salty.)

4

Filter step — catch the rice

Hold your strainer over a clean bowl. Slowly pour the water and rice through the strainer. The water passes through. The rice stays on top. Once all the water is poured, the strainer holds your rice and the bowl below holds the salty water.

Check your three piles

You should now have: paper clips (dish 1), rice (in the strainer or dish 2), and salty water (dish 3). The salt is still in the water but invisible. Place dish 3 in a sunny window — if you wait a day or two, the water will evaporate and salt crystals will appear at the bottom.

6

Make a claim

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In a mixture of rice, salt, and paper clips, why is the MAGNET step usually done first?
AIt's the most fun step.
BThe magnet only works on wet things.
CIt's clean and dry — nothing gets wet, so later steps are simpler.
✅ Correct! The magnet works on dry materials and keeps the clips clean. Once you add water, the clips would get wet and harder to separate cleanly.
❌ Not quite. Magnets actually work better on dry materials. The reason to go first is that nothing else gets disturbed yet.
After adding water to a mix of salt and rice, what do you see?
AThe rice dissolves and the salt stays solid.
BThe salt dissolves and the rice stays solid.
CBoth dissolve at the same speed.
✅ Correct! Salt is soluble in water; rice is not. The salt spreads through the water (you can't see it but it's there). The rice grains stay whole.
❌ Not quite. Think about Module 6 — which one is soluble in water? Salt particles get pulled apart by the water. Rice particles don't.
Your separation is done — three steps, three piles. How could you prove the salt is still in the leftover water?
ALet the water evaporate on a sunny windowsill — salt crystals appear at the bottom.
BPour it through the filter again — that would catch the salt.
CYou can't prove it — salt is gone for good once it dissolves.
✅ Correct! Evaporating the water leaves the salt behind. That's how sea salt is made on a much bigger scale. Matter is never lost — it just hides.
❌ Not quite. A filter can't catch dissolved salt — the particles are too small. And salt doesn't disappear; it just spread out. What if you removed the water?
