📖 The Lesson
Why the scale never lies about matter
⏱ 15 min
🛡️
Section 1
A Big Idea: Matter Is Conserved

One of the most important ideas in all of science: matter is never lost. Not when you cut it, melt it, dissolve it, or smash it into pieces. The form might change, but the massmassHow much stuff is in something. stays the same. This idea has a name: conservationconservationThe rule that matter is never lost in a change. of matter.

Why? Because when matter changes form, its particles just rearrange. They don't vanish. There's the exact same number of particles before AND after — they're just in different positions. So the total massmassHow much stuff is in something. can't change.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If you weigh a sandwich, then chew it up into a thousand pieces, do you have more, less, or the same amount of sandwich? Why?"
✂️
Section 2
Physical Changes — Same Matter, New Form

A physical changephysical changeA change in shape, size, or form — but the matter stays the same. is the kind of change you see every day. Cutting paper. Melting ice. Crumpling a soda can. Dissolving sugar in water. Tearing bread into bits.

In every physical change, the matter is still the same kind of stuff — just in a different shape or form. Ice and water and steam are all H₂O. Bread crumbs are still bread. A torn piece of paper is still paper. The mass stays the same.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"If you carefully cut an apple into 100 tiny pieces and weighed every piece together, the mass would be the same as before. But what if you ATE some of it — would the mass on the scale still be the same?"
📦
Section 3
Why "Closed" Containers Matter

There's one trick: matter is only conserved if it can't escape. Melt an ice cube in an open bowl, and the puddle might weigh slightly less days later — because water has slowly evaporated into the air. The matter isn't gone, it just left the bowl. The TOTAL mass of the world is still the same.

That's why scientists use closed containers for conservation tests — sealed bags, lidded cups, or sealed jars. Everything that was inside stays inside.

Real-World Connection: When chefs reduce a sauce on the stove, the sauce gets thicker not because anything was destroyed, but because water vapor evaporated out of the pot. If you'd put a lid on and weighed the pot, the mass would never have changed.

🔑 Key Concept

When matter changes form, no matter is lost. The mass before equals the mass after — as long as nothing escapes the container. Scales are the proof.

🤯
Mind-Blowing Fact
The conservation of matter rule was discovered around 1789 by a French scientist named Antoine Lavoisier. He used careful scales to prove it — and changed all of chemistry. Your work today follows the same method he invented over 230 years ago.
Go Deeper — Why does it LOOK like matter disappears?

Lots of things trick us into thinking matter is lost. A puddle dries up — but the water just turned to vapor and drifted into the air. A candle burns down — but the wax became gases that floated away. A piece of food rots — but the matter became gases plus tiny pieces eaten by microbes.

In every case, if you could collect ALL the gases and tiny pieces that left, the total mass would still match. Scientists know this because they can do exactly that experiment — and they get the same answer every time.

🔬 Predict the Scale
Three change scenarios. What does the scale show?
⏱ 12 min

Below are three change scenarios. For each one, you'll predict what the scale should read after the change. Then you'll explain WHY — using what you know about particles.

Scenario A

A piece of clay weighs 40 grams. You squash it flat, then roll it into a snake, then smash it into a ball. You weigh it again.

Scenario B

A sealed zip-top bag with an ice cube inside weighs 50 grams. You set it on the counter for an hour. The ice fully melts into water — still trapped in the sealed bag. You weigh it again.

Scenario C (tricky!)

An open cup of water with the same ice cube starts at 50 grams. You leave it out for THREE DAYS. The water level drops because some evaporated. You weigh it again.

Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
A 25-gram piece of clay is shaped into a snake, then a ball, then flattened. What does it weigh now?
ALess than 25 grams — some was lost
BExactly 25 grams — same particles, same mass
CMore than 25 grams — squashing added matter
✅ Correct! Shaping clay is a physical change. Every clay particle is still there — just in a different arrangement. The mass doesn't budge.
❌ Not quite. The shape changed, but no particles left or arrived. What stays the same when matter just changes form?
A sealed cup of water + salt weighs 200 g. After all the salt dissolves, what does the cup weigh?
ALess than 200 g — salt disappeared
BIt depends how long you stir
CExactly 200 g — salt particles are still in the cup, just spread out
✅ Correct! Dissolving is a physical change. The salt particles got pulled apart and spread through the water, but they're all still in the sealed cup.
❌ Not quite. The cup is sealed — nothing can leave. The salt didn't actually disappear; it just spread out. So the total mass…?
An OPEN pot of soup on the stove starts at 1,000 g. You simmer it for an hour. Now it weighs 850 g. Where did the mass go?
AAbout 150 g of water evaporated as steam into the kitchen air.
BThe matter was destroyed by heat.
CSoup molecules turn into less mass when heated.
✅ Correct! Matter is never actually destroyed. The 150 g of "missing" soup is now water vapor floating around your kitchen. If you could catch every bit, you'd find your 1,000 g.
❌ Not quite. Matter is never actually destroyed. The cup is open — so something MUST have left. What rises off boiling water?
