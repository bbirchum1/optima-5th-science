🔍 Notice, Wonder, and Predict
Same matter. Different form. Same mass?
⏱ 5 min

You push a swing forward and watch it go higher. You pull a wagon up a hill. You let go of a ball and it falls.

In each one, something CAUSED the motion. The swing didn't move on its own. The wagon didn't roll up the hill by itself. The ball didn't fall because it decided to.

Scientists call the cause of motion a force. Today you'll learn the three most common forces you see every day: pushes, pulls, and gravity.

📖 The Lesson
Energy comes in six familiar forms
⏱ 15 min
🛡️
Section 1
What Is a Force?

A force is a push or a pull. Forces cause things to move, stop, speed up, slow down, or change direction. Every time you see something change its motion, a force was involved.

Forces are everywhere. You use a push every time you press a button. You use a pull every time you open a drawer. Forces can be strong (kicking a ball) or gentle (turning a doorknob). They can come from your hands, from wind, from machines, or from the Earth itself.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Pick one motion happening near you right now. What force is causing it? Is it a push, a pull, or gravity?"
✂️
Section 2
Pushes and Pulls

A push moves an object AWAY from you. Pressing the gas pedal pushes the car forward. Pushing a swing sends a kid flying through the air. Pushing a heavy box across the floor takes a lot of force.

A pull moves an object TOWARD you (or to where you want it to go). Pulling a sled. Tugging on a leash. Opening a drawer. Even when you bring food to your mouth, you're pulling. Most pushes and pulls need contact — your hand touching the object.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"You're pulling a wagon up a hill. What if you let go? What's happening to the wagon now? What force takes over?"
📦
Section 3
Energy Causes Motion or Change

Gravity is a special force. It's the pull that Earth has on EVERY object — without touching them. Drop a pencil, and gravity pulls it down. Throw a ball up in the air, and gravity pulls it back. Even right now, gravity is pulling you down onto your chair.

Gravity is the reason things fall DOWN instead of in some other direction. Earth is the biggest thing nearby, so its pull is the strongest pull on you. Gravity is also why a thrown ball comes back, why rain falls, and why you don't float off into space.

Real-World Connection: Astronauts in space stations experience much less gravity from Earth because they're very far from its center. They float around inside the station! When they return to Earth, gravity pulls them down hard — they have to retrain their bodies to walk.

🔑 Key Concept

A force is a push or a pull. Pushes and pulls usually need contact, but gravity is a pull that works without touching. Forces cause objects to start, stop, or change direction.

Mind-Blowing Fact
If you weigh 80 pounds on Earth, you'd weigh only about 13 pounds on the Moon. Why? The Moon is much smaller than Earth, so its gravity is much weaker. Your mass (how much stuff is in you) doesn't change — but your weight (how hard gravity pulls you) does.
Go Deeper — Is friction a force too?

Yes! Friction is a force that opposes motion. When you slide a book across a table, friction pushes BACK on the book and slows it down. If there were no friction, things would never stop moving once started. Hockey pucks slide far on ice because ice has very little friction.

Friction can be helpful or annoying. It helps your shoes grip the floor so you don't slip. It helps brakes stop a bike. But it also wears out tires and slows things down. We'll explore friction more in upcoming lessons.

🔬 Spot the Force
Three scenes. What force is making things move?
⏱ 12 min

Below are three motion scenes. For each one, identify the force causing the motion. Is it a push, a pull, or gravity?

Scenario A

Scene A. A kid is on a swing. A parent steps behind and presses on the kid's back. The swing moves forward. The parent does this several times.

Scenario B

Scene B. A book sits on a shelf. You knock it off the edge. It falls straight down to the floor. Nothing else touched it on the way down.

Scenario C

Scene C. A kid tugs on a wagon's handle. The wagon rolls behind them across the grass. They have to keep tugging to keep it moving.

Spot what's in contact

Pushes and pulls usually need contact (touching). Gravity does not. For each scene, ask: is the object being touched by something? If yes, it's probably a push or pull. If no, it's probably gravity.
