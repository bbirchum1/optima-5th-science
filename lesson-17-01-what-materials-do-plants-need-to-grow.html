🔍 Notice, Wonder, and Predict
Same seed. A whole tree. Where did the rest come from?
⏱ 5 min

A tiny apple seed weighs about a thousandth of an ounce. Plant it. Water it. Wait a few decades. Now you have an apple tree — a few hundred pounds of wood, branches, leaves, and fruit.

The seed weighed almost nothing. The tree weighs thousands of times more. Where did all the new wood, leaves, and bark come from? Most people guess the soil — they assume the plant "ate" dirt. But scientists figured out something different a long time ago.

📖 The Lesson
The surprising answer scientists found
⏱ 15 min
👁️
Section 1
The Soil Misconception

For a long time, people thought plants ate the soil. The plant sat in dirt — and the plant grew bigger — so the dirt must be what filled it up, right? It seemed obvious. But scientists tested it carefully, and the answer turned out to be wrong.

In 1648, a Dutch scientist named Jan van Helmont weighed a small willow tree (5 pounds) and the soil in its pot (200 pounds). He watered the tree for 5 years and never added soil. After 5 years, the tree weighed 169 pounds — but the soil only lost about 2 ounces. Where did the other 164 pounds of tree come from? Not the soil!

🤔
Stop and Think
"Look at the sentence: 'The puddle is bigger than it was yesterday.' Is that an observation or an inference? What about: 'It must have rained last night'?"
🧾
Section 2
Why Evidence Matters

EvidenceevidenceObservations or data that support or test an idea. is what scientists use to back up their ideas. The more careful your observations, the stronger your evidence becomes. A scientist doesn't say, "I think the leaves are turning yellow." They write down the dates, the colors, and the counts — so anyone could check.

This is why plants are called PRODUCERS. They produce (make) their own food from the materials around them. Animals can't do this — animals have to eat plants (or other animals). Producers are the foundation of life everywhere.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"Imagine two friends both say a plant is dying. One says, 'It just looks sad.' The other says, 'Six of its eight leaves are brown, and the soil has been dry for five days.' Whose claim is stronger, and why?"
❓
Section 3
Asking Testable Questions

Scientists don't ask just any question — they ask testable questionstestable questionA question you can answer by observing, measuring, or testing.. "Why are clouds beautiful?" isn't testable. But "Do clouds form more often in the morning or the afternoon?" — that's something you could actually find out by watching the sky for a week.

Real-World Connection: Without plants, animals (including us) wouldn't have food OR oxygen. Plants pull CO₂ out of the air and release O₂ — the gas we breathe. They're also the start of every food chain. We eat plants directly, or we eat animals that ate plants. Everything alive depends on what plants do.

🔑 Key Concept

Plants get most of their material from AIR (carbon dioxide) and WATER — not from the soil. Roots pull in water. Leaves pull in air. Sunlight gives the energy. Together, they build the plant's body.

Mind-Blowing Fact
Most of the WOOD in a tree was once invisible gas floating in the air! Plants pull carbon dioxide out of the atmosphere and lock it into wood, leaves, and bark. A 100-pound tree has about 50 pounds of carbon that came from the air around it.
Go Deeper — Why scientists still use paper notebooks

The plant's job — making food from air, water, and sunlight — is called PHOTOSYNTHESIS. You don't need to memorize that big word yet, but here's the cool part: photosynthesis is how almost ALL the food on Earth gets started. Even the meat you eat came from animals that ate plants that did photosynthesis.

If you visited a research station today, you'd probably find shelves full of yellow waterproof notebooks. Some are 30 or 40 years old, still being checked when scientists need to compare today's data to data from a long time ago.

2

Look for the pattern

Look at the notes you marked as observations. What do they have in common? Now look at the inferences. What words tip you off — words like "must," "probably," or "because"?

3

Turn one inference into a testable question

Pick one of Sam's inferences. Rewrite it as a testable question — one a person could answer by watching, measuring, or testing. Example: "Do lizards stay still when it gets hotter?"

4

Make a claim

Based on what you noticed, write a one-sentence claim about why scientists keep observations and inferences in separate columns. You'll use this idea in your CER later.

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
Where does MOST of a plant's material come from?
AThe soil — plants eat the dirt around them
BAir (carbon dioxide) and water
CSunlight gets stored in the plant as solid material
✅ Correct! Most of a plant's material comes from CO₂ in the air and water from the soil. The plant uses sunlight as energy to combine them.
❌ Not quite. Soil only loses a tiny amount of mass even after years of plant growth. Sunlight is energy, not solid material. So what's actually building the plant?
Which plant part pulls in carbon dioxide from the air?
AThe roots
BThe stem
CThe leaves
✅ Correct! Leaves have tiny holes called stomata that pull in CO₂ from the air. The leaves are the plant's "lungs" — air comes in here.
❌ Not quite. Roots are in the soil pulling water. Stems carry things between leaves and roots. Which part is actually exposed to the air?
A scientist weighs a pot of soil + a small willow tree (5 lb tree, 200 lb soil). After 5 years of just watering it, the tree weighs 169 lb but the soil only lost 2 oz. Where did the new tree material come from?
AMostly from air (CO₂) and water
BFrom the 2 oz of soil that disappeared
CFrom sunlight turning into wood
✅ Correct! This is the actual experiment Jan van Helmont ran in 1648. Only 2 oz of soil were lost, but 164 lb of tree was gained. The rest came from air and water.
❌ Not quite. 2 oz of soil cannot become 164 lb of tree. And sunlight is energy, not solid stuff. What gases and liquids did the plant pull in?
