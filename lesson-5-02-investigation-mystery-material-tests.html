🔍 Notice, Wonder, and Predict
Two metals. One magnet. Different results.
⏱ 5 min

Here's a trick that surprises most people: a paper clip sticks to a magnet, but a penny doesn't. Both are metals. Both are shiny. Both feel cold. But only one is magnetic.

That's not a mistake. It's because "metal" isn't one single material — there are many kinds, and only some are magnetic. Today you're going to find out which is which.

📖 The Four Tests
A short guide before you start testing
⏱ 7 min
🧲
Test 1 & 2
Magnet, Then Scratch

The magnetmagneticA material a magnet sticks to. test is fastest. Hold your magnet near each object. Either the magnet sticks or it doesn't — write YES or NO. Most metals you'll find at home are NOT magnetic. Only iron and steel reliably stick.

Next, try the hardnesshardnessHow hard it is to scratch a material. test. Press your fingernail across each object. If your nail leaves a mark, write "soft." If it doesn't, the object is harder than your nail — write "hard."

🤔
Stop and Think
"A penny passes the hardness test (your nail can't scratch it) but fails the magnet test. What does that combination tell you?"
🌊
Test 3 & 4
Float, Then Look

Drop each object gently into your bowl of water. Does it sink or floatsink or floatWhether an object goes under or stays on top.? Things heavier than the same amount of water sink. Most metals and rocks sink. Most plastic and wood float.

The last test is just looking carefully. Is it shiny or dull? What color? Are there patterns, marks, or letters? Visual clues can confirm what the other tests suggest. A shiny silver-gray that doesn't stick to a magnet and sinks heavy? That's probably aluminum or zinc — not iron.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"If two of your objects both sink, can you tell them apart from the float test alone? What test would you need to add?"
📋
Section 3
Use the Whole Pattern, Not Just One Test

No single test is enough. A coin doesn't stick to a magnet AND it sinks. That alone could match copper, aluminum, or zinc. But add "shiny, dull-orange, small, hard" and you've got copper. The whole pattern of four answers is what identifies the material.

Real-World Connection: When archaeologists dig up an old metal object, they run dozens of property tests before they label it. One test isn't proof — but a matching pattern is.

🔑 Key Concept

Four quick tests — magnet, scratch, float, look — give you a pattern of property results. The pattern, not any single test, is what identifies a material.

Mind-Blowing Fact
Recycling factories use the same four tests you'll do today — only their magnets can be over 6 feet wide and can pull tons of steel from a stream of mixed trash in seconds.
Go Deeper — Why aren't all metals magnetic?

Only a few metals — iron, nickel, cobalt — and the alloys made from them (like steel) are strongly magnetic. The reason has to do with how the particles inside them are arranged: they can line up like tiny compass needles. Copper, aluminum, gold, and silver have a different inner arrangement, so a magnet has no effect.

This is why a US nickel doesn't stick to a magnet, even though it's named after a metal that does. Modern nickels are mostly copper — only 25% nickel — and the copper wins.

🔬 The Mystery Lab
Sixteen tests. Four identifications. One detective: you.
⏱ 25 min

Gather your four samples and your tools. Work over a tray or on a counter near the sink (the water test gets things damp). You'll be doing 16 small tests in total — but each one only takes a few seconds.

⚠️

Before you start: Use cool water from the tap. Keep your magnet away from screens, credit cards, and computers — magnets can mess with the strips and chips inside those.

1

Test 1 — Magnet

Hold your magnet just above each object, one at a time. If the object jumps up or sticks, write YES. If nothing happens, write NO. Move on quickly.

2

Test 2 — Scratch

Press your fingernail firmly across the surface of each object. If your nail leaves any kind of mark or dent, write SOFT. If your nail just slides over with no mark, write HARD.

3

Test 3 — Float

Gently drop each object into the bowl of water. If it goes to the bottom, write SINKS. If it stays on top, write FLOATS. Dry each object with a paper towel before moving on.

4

Test 4 — Look

Look closely at each object. Write its color, whether it's shiny or dull, and one more visual detail (texture, pattern, words, anything you notice).

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
A paper clip and a penny are both shiny metals. Which test would tell them apart fastest?
AThe scratch test (both are hard, so this won't help)
BThe magnet test — only one of them sticks
CThe float test (both will sink, same answer)
✅ Correct! Steel (the paper clip) is magnetic. Copper alloy (most coins) isn't. The magnet test separates them instantly.
❌ Not quite. Both objects are hard and both sink, so those tests don't separate them. Which test gives different answers for the two?
Your object: doesn't stick to a magnet, your fingernail can scratch it, and it floats. What's the best identification?
ASteel — it doesn't stick.
BRock — it floats.
CPlastic — those three results match plastic's fingerprint.
✅ Correct! Steel would stick to the magnet. Rocks usually sink and can't be scratched. Soft + floats + non-magnetic is plastic's signature pattern.
❌ Not quite. Use the WHOLE pattern. Steel would stick to the magnet (it doesn't). Rocks usually sink (this floats). Which material is soft AND light AND non-magnetic?
Why is it useful to do FOUR tests instead of just one when identifying a material?
AFour tests together create a unique pattern — one test alone can be misleading.
BScientists always do exactly four tests, no more and no less.
CDoing more tests wastes time and gives the same answer.
✅ Correct! Different materials can match on one or two tests, but the full pattern of four answers is much more unique. That's how you avoid being fooled.
❌ Not quite. The number 4 isn't magic — but multiple tests together let you spot lookalikes. Which option explains why?
