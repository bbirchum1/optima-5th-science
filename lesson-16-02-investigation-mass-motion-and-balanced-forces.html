📖 Before You Begin
Safety check and the three-test plan
⏱ 7 min
⚖️
Section 1
Safety First

Today's two experiments use simple home items — two objects of different mass (a tennis ball and a heavy book), a smooth flat surface, and a piece of string about 24 inches long. The setup is safe but a few rules apply. Don't push objects toward people or breakables. Don't use a string near anyone's neck. Do work with a partner for the tug-of-war.

Have a parent or older helper nearby for Test A in case the paper ball flies somewhere unexpected. Clean up your testing area afterward.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Why is it important that you push BOTH objects in the mass test with the SAME force?"
📦
Section 2
The Three Tests at a Glance

You'll run two experiments. Experiment 1 — Mass Test: push a light object and a heavy object with the SAME force. Measure how far each one moves. Experiment 2 — Tug of War: with a partner, pull equally on a string. Then pull unequally. Observe what happens.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"Which of the three tests do you think will give you the most obvious evidence of energy at work?"
📊
Section 3
Recording What You See, Hear, and Feel

For each of your three tests, you'll fill out a row of a table. You'll record three things: what kind of energy you START with, what kind of motion or change you END with, and what evidence you saw, heard, or felt.

Real-World Connection: Engineers test energy all the time. They make sure cars convert chemical energy in fuel into mechanical energy that moves the wheels. They make sure speakers turn electrical energy into sound. They always start with what should change — and they collect evidence to prove it did.

🔑 Key Concept

To prove energy was at work, look for what changed. Did something move? Did something warm up? Did something make a sound? Each is strong evidence of energy doing its job.

Mind-Blowing Fact
The sound from your tapped glass is actually thousands of vibrations per second. Your ear catches each one and your brain turns it into the "ring" you hear — all in a fraction of a second. The glass is shaking faster than you can blink.
Go Deeper — What if your flashlight is dim?

If your flashlight isn't very bright, the dark paper might warm up only a tiny bit. You can still feel the difference if you wait longer — try 2 or 3 minutes instead of 60 seconds. Or move the flashlight even closer (just don't let the lens touch the paper).

Another option: use sunlight from a window. Place dark and light paper next to each other in a sunny spot. After 5–10 minutes, compare how warm each feels. The sun is one of the brightest light sources you have — and the dark paper will get clearly warmer than the light one.

Step 1 — Set Up the Mass Test

Place a light object (tennis ball, paper cup) and a heavy object (textbook, full water bottle) several feet apart on a smooth floor or table. Make sure each has room to roll/slide.

2

Test B — Flashlight Heat

Place a piece of dark paper on a table. Turn on a flashlight and hold it 2-3 inches above the paper, beam pointing down. Wait 60 seconds. Carefully touch the spot where the beam was hitting. Now touch a different spot the beam was NOT on. Write down what you felt.

3

Test C — Glass Bell

Place a clean glass on the table. Hold a metal spoon by the handle. Gently tap the side of the glass. Listen. Write down what you heard. Try tapping a different part of the glass — does the sound change?

4

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In your mass test, which object moved farther with the same force?
AThe heavy object — it always moves more
BThe light object — same force has a bigger effect on less mass
CBoth move exactly the same distance
✅ Correct! The lighter object moves more because the same force has a bigger effect on it. Less mass = more reaction to the same force.
❌ Not quite. Mass resists motion — that's the whole rule. A bigger mass with the same force = less motion, not more or the same.
In a tug-of-war, both teams pull with EQUAL force. What happens to the rope?
AIt stays still — balanced forces cancel out
BIt moves toward the team with stronger hands
CIt breaks
✅ Correct! Equal pulls in opposite directions = balanced forces. The rope stays still because the two forces cancel each other out.
❌ Not quite. If the forces are EQUAL, neither side wins. The rope stays still until something changes.
Your friend pushes a small toy car with one finger and it rolls 10 feet. They push a real car with the same finger. What happens?
AThe real car doesn't budge — its mass is too big for one finger's force
BThe real car rolls 10 feet too
CThe real car rolls 5 feet
✅ Correct! A real car has thousands of times more mass than a toy car. The same finger force has almost no effect on something that big.
❌ Not quite. A real car has way more mass than a toy car. The same finger force isn't big enough to move that much mass.
