📖 The Lesson
What scientists actually do — and how you'll do it too
⏱ 15 min
👁️
Section 1
Observation vs. Inference

An observationobservationSomething you notice using your senses or measurements. is something you can see, hear, smell, count, or measure. "The shell has two cracks." That's an observation. "Someone dropped this shell here." That's not. That's an inferenceinferenceAn idea about what an observation means. — a guess about what your observations mean.

Both are useful. But scientists keep them separate, because mixing them up can lead to wrong answers. A shell with two cracks could have been dropped — or it could have been bumped by waves, or stepped on by a bird. Without more evidence, you can't be sure.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Look at the sentence: 'The puddle is bigger than it was yesterday.' Is that an observation or an inference? What about: 'It must have rained last night'?"
🧾
Section 2
Why Evidence Matters

EvidenceevidenceObservations or data that support or test an idea. is what scientists use to back up their ideas. The more careful your observations, the stronger your evidence becomes. A scientist doesn't say, "I think the leaves are turning yellow." They write down the dates, the colors, and the counts — so anyone could check.

When you base an idea on evidence, you can defend it. When you base it on a guess, someone can easily push back. That's why a scientist's notebook is full of numbers, dates, sketches, and careful words. Each one becomes evidence later.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"Imagine two friends both say a plant is dying. One says, 'It just looks sad.' The other says, 'Six of its eight leaves are brown, and the soil has been dry for five days.' Whose claim is stronger, and why?"
❓
Section 3
Asking Testable Questions

Scientists don't ask just any question — they ask testable questionstestable questionA question you can answer by observing, measuring, or testing.. "Why are clouds beautiful?" isn't testable. But "Do clouds form more often in the morning or the afternoon?" — that's something you could actually find out by watching the sky for a week.

Real-World Connection: Park rangers and field biologists keep notebooks just like the ones you'll use this year. Every careful observation they write down today might become evidence in a study someone uses ten years from now.

🔑 Key Concept

A scientific observation is something you can measure, count, or describe carefully. An inference is what you think the observation means. Scientists keep them separate so their evidence stays strong.

Mind-Blowing Fact
Charles Darwin kept field notebooks for almost 50 years. Some of his sketches and notes from a single beetle on a single afternoon are still being used by scientists today — almost 200 years later.
Go Deeper — Why scientists still use paper notebooks

You'd think modern scientists would type everything on a computer. Many do — eventually. But field notebooks are still mostly paper. Why? They don't run out of battery. They survive rain (especially the waterproof kind). And they're easy to flip through years later.

If you visited a research station today, you'd probably find shelves full of yellow waterproof notebooks. Some are 30 or 40 years old, still being checked when scientists need to compare today's data to data from a long time ago.

Observation or inference? You decide.

Read Sam's notebook

Read each of Sam's six notes below. For each one, decide if it is an observation or an inference. Don't rush — look closely at the words Sam chose.

📋 Sam's notebook — copy this into your own notebook
# What Sam wrote Observation or Inference?
1"The lizard is brown with darker stripes on its back."
2"It must be hot, because the lizard is staying still."
3"It is about 4 inches long, from nose to tail."
4"The lizard is scared of me."
5"It blinked twice in ten seconds."
6"It probably eats bugs from the garden."
2

Look for the pattern

Look at the notes you marked as observations. What do they have in common? Now look at the inferences. What words tip you off — words like "must," "probably," or "because"?

3

Turn one inference into a testable question

Pick one of Sam's inferences. Rewrite it as a testable question — one a person could answer by watching, measuring, or testing. Example: "Do lizards stay still when it gets hotter?"

4

Make a claim

Based on what you noticed, write a one-sentence claim about why scientists keep observations and inferences in separate columns. You'll use this idea in your CER later.

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
Which sentence below is an observation, not an inference?
A"The dog looks happy."
B"The dog is wagging its tail."
C"The dog probably wants to play."
✅ Correct! "Wagging its tail" is something you can see and count — a clean observation. The other two are guesses about what the dog is feeling.
❌ Not quite. Look for the sentence that describes only what you can see, with no guessing involved. The words "happy" and "probably" are clues.
Which question below is a testable question?
A"Do paper airplanes fly farther when they have a paper clip on the nose?"
B"Are paper airplanes more fun than kites?"
C"Why are paper airplanes the best?"
✅ Correct! You could actually test that — throw planes with and without paper clips and measure the distance each one flies.
❌ Not quite. A testable question can be answered by collecting data or running a test. Which one could you set up an experiment for?
A scientist writes: "The plant grew 2 cm in three days. It must like its new spot." Which part is the inference?
A"The plant grew 2 cm."
B"…in three days."
C"It must like its new spot."
✅ Correct! That part is a guess about what the growth means. The measurement is the observation; "must like" is the inference.
❌ Not quite. The numbers are observations — they can be measured. Look for the part that explains why, not what.
