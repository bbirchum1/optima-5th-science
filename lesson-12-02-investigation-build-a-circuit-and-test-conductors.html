Big Question
Think About This…

Can you prove with a real scale that matter is conserved — even when it changes form right in front of you?

Keep this question in mind during your lab.

📖 Before You Begin
Safety check and the three-test plan
⏱ 7 min
⚖️
Section 1
Safety First

Today's three tests use simple home materials. They are safe, but you should still be careful. Test A: A stretched rubber band can sting if it hits skin — aim AWAY from people, animals, and breakables. Test B: Don't shine the flashlight in your eyes. Test C: Tap gently — a hard tap can chip or crack glass.

Have a parent or older helper nearby for Test A in case the paper ball flies somewhere unexpected. Clean up your testing area afterward.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Why is it smart to aim the rubber band AWAY from people, even if you think it can't fly far?"
📦
Section 2
The Three Tests at a Glance

You'll run three small tests. Test A — Rubber Band Launcher: stretch a rubber band on a ruler and use it to fling a small paper ball. Test B — Flashlight Heat: shine a flashlight close to a piece of dark paper for 60 seconds. Test C — Glass Bell: gently tap a drinking glass with a metal spoon.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"Which of the three tests do you think will give you the most obvious evidence of energy at work?"
📊
Section 3
Recording What You See, Hear, and Feel

For each of your three tests, you'll fill out a row of a table. You'll record three things: what kind of energy you START with, what kind of motion or change you END with, and what evidence you saw, heard, or felt.

Real-World Connection: Engineers test energy all the time. They make sure cars convert chemical energy in fuel into mechanical energy that moves the wheels. They make sure speakers turn electrical energy into sound. They always start with what should change — and they collect evidence to prove it did.

🔑 Key Concept

To prove energy was at work, look for what changed. Did something move? Did something warm up? Did something make a sound? Each is strong evidence of energy doing its job.

🗺 Tap each test to see the energy at work

Three small tests. Each one shows energy causing a different result — motion, heat, or sound. Tap each for the steps.

👆

Tap each trial for the full procedure.

Three tests. Three forms of evidence. One conclusion: energy causes motion or change.
🤯
Mind-Blowing Fact
The sound from your tapped glass is actually thousands of vibrations per second. Your ear catches each one and your brain turns it into the "ring" you hear — all in a fraction of a second. The glass is shaking faster than you can blink.
Go Deeper — What if your flashlight is dim?

If your flashlight isn't very bright, the dark paper might warm up only a tiny bit. You can still feel the difference if you wait longer — try 2 or 3 minutes instead of 60 seconds. Or move the flashlight even closer (just don't let the lens touch the paper).

Another option: use sunlight from a window. Place dark and light paper next to each other in a sunny spot. After 5–10 minutes, compare how warm each feels. The sun is one of the brightest light sources you have — and the dark paper will get clearly warmer than the light one.

Gather your materials. You'll need: a thin rubber band (the kind you can stretch easily), a ruler, a small piece of paper (crumple into a tiny ball), a flashlight, one piece of dark-colored paper, a drinking glass, and a metal spoon.

1

Test A — Rubber Band Launcher

Stretch a thin rubber band lengthwise across one end of a ruler so it acts like a tiny slingshot. Place a small crumpled paper ball in front of the band. Aim AWAY from people and breakables. Pull the band back slightly, then let go. Write down what happened. Did the paper ball move? How far?

2

Test B — Flashlight Heat

Place a piece of dark paper on a table. Turn on a flashlight and hold it 2-3 inches above the paper, beam pointing down. Wait 60 seconds. Carefully touch the spot where the beam was hitting. Now touch a different spot the beam was NOT on. Write down what you felt.

3

Test C — Glass Bell

Place a clean glass on the table. Hold a metal spoon by the handle. Gently tap the side of the glass. Listen. Write down what you heard. Try tapping a different part of the glass — does the sound change?

4

Compare your results

Look back at all three tests. Each one started with energy in some form. Each one ended with motion, warming, or sound. Write down which form of energy started each test and what kind of change came out.

Find the common pattern

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
When you opened the flashlight, which part was the power source?
AThe bulb
BThe battery
CThe metal spring
✅ Correct! The battery is the power source — it stores chemical energy that becomes electrical energy. The spring connects the battery to the bulb. The bulb is what the electricity powers.
❌ Not quite. The bulb USES electricity but doesn't make it. The spring CONNECTS, but doesn't store power. What part inside is full of chemical energy waiting to be released?
A paperclip and a rubber band are both small. Which one would best replace a flashlight's spring?
AThe paperclip — it's a metal conductor
BThe rubber band — it's flexible like a spring
CEither one would work the same
✅ Correct! A paperclip is metal — electricity can flow through it. A rubber band is an insulator — electricity can't flow through it. The paperclip would complete the circuit; the rubber band would break it.
❌ Not quite. Flexibility doesn't matter for a circuit. What matters is whether electricity can flow through the material. Rubber is an insulator — it blocks electricity.
Surprise fact: pencil graphite (the dark "lead") can actually conduct electricity. What would happen if you put a thick line of pencil graphite between the battery and the bulb in a circuit?
AThe bulb would light up — graphite acts like a conductor
BNothing would happen — pencils are wood
CThe bulb would explode
✅ Correct! Graphite is a conductor (it's a form of carbon, like coal). Electricity can flow right through a thick pencil line and complete a circuit — though slower than through metal.
❌ Not quite. The pencil "lead" is actually graphite — a type of carbon that conducts electricity. The wood around it is the insulator, but graphite itself works as a wire.
