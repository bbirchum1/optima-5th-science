📖 Three Tests You'll Run
Before you start, here's how each test works.
⏱ 8 min
🔷
Test 1
Does It Keep Its Own Shape?

Hold up each sample and look at it. Move it to a different container. Did the shape change?

A solidsolidMatter that keeps its own shape. always looks the same — a rock is rock-shaped in your hand and rock-shaped in the bowl. A liquidliquidMatter that takes the shape of its container. changes shape every time you pour it. A gasgasMatter that fills any container it's in. fills the whole bag — including the corners.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If you squeeze the air bag, the air still fills every corner. Why?"
📐
Test 2
Does the Amount Stay the Same?

Take each sample and put it in two different containers. Watch the volumevolumeHow much space something takes up. — the total amount of space it takes up.

A solid keeps the exact same volume — your rock is the same size no matter where it goes. A liquid keeps the same amount even when it changes shape — half a cup is half a cup. But a gas? A gas spreads to fill whatever container it's in. The bag holds it in, but if you opened the bag in a big room, the air would spread everywhere.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"If you poured ½ cup of water into a bigger cup, would it still be ½ cup of water? How do you know?"
🌊
Test 3
Does It Flow?

Try to pour each sample. The rock won't pour — it slides or thunks. The water flows easily from cup to cup. The air? You can't see it flow, but you can feel it — squeeze the bag and you'll feel the gas pushing the bag wherever there's room.

Real-World Connection: The same three tests work for any matter, anywhere. Even matter scientists have never seen before. That's what makes properties so powerful — they're how we sort everything.

🔑 Key Concept

Three tests — shape, volume, flow — work together to tell solids, liquids, and gases apart. The pattern of YES/NO answers is what gives away the state of matter every time.

🤯
Mind-Blowing Fact
The air inside your sealed bag has mass. A bag with about 1 liter of air weighs roughly 1.2 grams more than the same bag with no air at all. You're holding matter you can't see.
Go Deeper — What if a test gives you mixed results?

Sometimes a sample fails one test but passes the others. Sand, for example, can pour like a liquid, but each grain keeps its own shape. So a scientist would say sand is a "solid" — but it acts strange because the pieces are very small.

That's why running all three tests matters more than running just one. The full pattern of YES/NO answers is way more reliable than any single test.

🔬 Test All Three Samples
Three samples × three tests = nine little experiments.
⏱ 25 min

Gather your three samples on a clear table. Make sure your sandwich bag is full of air and zipped shut. Have two different containers (cups, bowls, or jars) ready for the pouring tests.

⚠️

Before you start: Your "solid" can be anything small and not fragile — a coin, an eraser, a pebble. Use plain cool water from the tap. Work over the sink or a tray so spills aren't a problem.

1

Test 1: Shape

Move each sample into your two different containers. After each move, write down whether the sample keeps its own shape, or whether it changes shape to match the container. Watch the air bag — even sealed, the air shifts around.

2

Test 2: Volume

Decide whether the amount of each sample changes when you move it. Did the rock get bigger or smaller? Did you somehow get more water? Did the air bag puff out the same in both containers?

3

Test 3: Flow

Try pouring each sample (gently!) between your containers. For the air, keep the bag sealed and gently squeeze it — you'll feel the gas push back. Record what each sample does.

Quick Self-Check

You test a mystery sample. It keeps its shape AND keeps its volume. What state is it?
AGas
BLiquid
CSolid
✅ Correct! A solid is the only state that passes BOTH the shape test and the volume test. It doesn't change either one.
❌ Not quite. Look back at the roadmap. Which state gets a YES on shape AND a YES on volume?
A sample takes the shape of every container you put it in, but always keeps the same amount. What is it?
AA liquid — it changes shape but keeps its amount.
BA solid — it keeps the same shape and amount.
CA gas — it always spreads out.
✅ Correct! That's the signature of a liquid: shape changes but amount stays put. Half a cup is always half a cup.
❌ Not quite. A solid wouldn't change shape. A gas wouldn't keep the same amount in different containers. Which state has both?
You squeeze your sealed air bag. The bag changes shape, but the air pushes back. What does that prove?
AThe bag is empty — air isn't matter.
BThe air is real matter that takes up space, even if you can't see it.
CThe bag is the matter — the air doesn't matter.
✅ Correct! If the air weren't matter, the bag would just collapse. The push-back is your evidence — gas takes up real space.
❌ Not quite. Empty bags collapse. The air pushing back proves it's there — and that means it's matter.
