📖 Before You Begin
Safety check and the three-test plan
⏱ 7 min
⚖️
Section 1
Safety First

Today's three tests use everyday objects — small toys, a marble or ball, paper, and a string. They are safe but a few rules apply. Don't drop heavy or breakable things. Don't climb on furniture to drop things from high up — ask an adult to help. Be careful not to hit anyone or anything fragile.

The drops in Test A only need to be about 3 feet (the height of a kitchen counter). The ramp in Test B can be made from a board, a book, or even a magazine. The hang test in Test C needs a string about 12 inches long and a small weight (a coin, a small toy).

🤔
Stop and Think
"Why should you choose small, soft objects for the drop test instead of heavy or sharp ones?"
📦
Section 2
The Three Tests at a Glance

You'll run three quick tests. Test A — Drop: drop two different objects from the same height. Watch how each falls. Test B — Ramp Roll: tilt a ramp and let a small ball roll down. Test C — Hang: tie a small weight to a string and watch which way it points.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"Which test do you think will be the most clear evidence of gravity? Which might be trickiest to set up?"
📊
Section 3
Recording Your Evidence

For each test, you'll fill out a row of a table. Three columns: the test, what you observed, and what it tells you about gravity. Sketch each test in your notebook with arrows showing the direction of motion.

Real-World Connection: Scientists do gravity experiments like yours in much bigger ways. NASA tests how things behave in low gravity using special airplanes that fly in steep curves — for about 20 seconds at the top of the curve, everything inside feels weightless. They use this to test how astronauts and equipment will work in space.

🔑 Key Concept

Every object you let go of falls DOWN. Balls roll DOWN ramps. Hanging weights point DOWN. The pattern is the evidence: gravity always pulls objects toward Earth.

Mind-Blowing Fact
In 1971, astronaut David Scott dropped a hammer and a feather on the moon at the same time. They hit the ground together! On the moon, there's almost no air to slow the feather. Both fall purely because of gravity. On Earth, air resistance is the only reason a feather falls slower than a hammer.
Go Deeper — Why does air slow some things and not others?

Air resistance depends on shape and surface area. A flat piece of paper has lots of surface for air to push against, so it floats and drifts. A crumpled piece of paper has much less surface — it falls almost as fast as a marble. Try this yourself: drop a flat piece of paper and a crumpled one at the same time.

Parachutes work because of this. The wide chute creates lots of air resistance, slowing the person down so they don't hit the ground too hard. Skydivers control their speed by spreading their arms (more air resistance) or pulling them in (less).

Your three gravity-test setups. A marble and paper for the drop test, a book ramp for the roll test, a hanging string for the direction test.
1

Test A — Drop Two Objects

Hold a small marble in one hand and a flat piece of paper in the other, both at the same height (counter or table height). Make sure no one is below. Let go of BOTH at the same time. Watch which hits the floor first. Write down what you see.

2

Test B — Roll Down a Ramp

Place a piece of dark paper on a table. Turn on a flashlight and hold it 2-3 inches above the paper, beam pointing down. Wait 60 seconds. Carefully touch the spot where the beam was hitting. Now touch a different spot the beam was NOT on. Write down what you felt.

3

Test C — Hang a Weight

Tie a small weight (coin, washer, or small toy) to one end of a string. Hold the other end up. Watch which way the string points. Now SLOWLY rotate your body in a circle while still holding the string. Does the string still point the same way? Write down what you observe.

4

Look for the pattern

All three tests should show motion in the SAME direction. What direction? Look at each result: where did things fall, roll, or point? Write down the pattern.

Sketch each test in your notebook: Show each test with arrows pointing in the direction of motion. Every arrow should point DOWN. Sketches are strong evidence — circle the direction arrows in red.

5

Compare your three results

All three tests should show the same big pattern. Write it in one sentence: "In all three tests, the motion was ___."

6

Write your claim

Write one sentence: "Across all three tests, gravity pulled objects ___." This will become part of your CER.

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In Test A, both the marble and the paper fell DOWN. Why?
AThey were both pulled down by gravity
BThey both wanted to land on the floor
CThe floor pulled them
✅ Correct! Gravity pulls every object toward Earth. The marble and paper both fall down because Earth is pulling on them.
❌ Not quite. Objects don't "want" to fall, and the floor doesn't reach up to pull things. What invisible force pulls everything DOWN?
In Test B, a steeper ramp made the ball roll faster. Why?
ASteeper ramps let gravity pull more directly on the ball, making it roll faster
BSteeper ramps are smoother
CThe ramp is pushing the ball
✅ Correct! On a steep ramp, more of gravity's downward pull goes into making the ball move along the slope. On a gentle slope, most of gravity's pull is straight down — the ball moves slower along the path.
❌ Not quite. The ramp doesn't push the ball, and smoothness doesn't make a big difference here. What invisible force is pulling the ball down?
In Test C, the hanging weight always pointed DOWN — no matter which way you faced. Why?
AGravity always pulls toward the center of Earth, which is straight down through the floor
BThe weight is magnetic and is pulled by the ground
CThe string is shaped to only point down
✅ Correct! Gravity points toward the center of Earth from anywhere on the planet. The string hangs in the direction of that pull — always straight DOWN, no matter how you rotate.
❌ Not quite. The weight isn't magnetic, and the string's shape doesn't force a direction. What pulls EVERYTHING toward Earth's center?
