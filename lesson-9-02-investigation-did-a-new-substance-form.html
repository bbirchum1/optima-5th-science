📖 Before You Mix
Quick safety check and the three-test plan
⏱ 7 min
⚖️
Section 1
Safety First

Today's mixing tests are safe — they use ordinary kitchen ingredients. But you should still be careful. Vinegar can sting your eyes, so don't lean over the cup when you pour. The fizzing test can foam up over the cup, so work over the sink or on a paper towel. Don't taste or drink any of the mixtures.

Wash your hands when you're done. Pour the mixtures down the sink and rinse your cups. If anything spills on the counter, wipe it up before it dries.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Why is it smart to do the fizzing test over the sink instead of on your kitchen table?"
📦
Section 2
The Three Tests

You'll run three small tests. Test A: stir 1 spoon of salt into about half a cup of cool water. Test B: drop 1 spoon of baking soda into about a quarter cup of vinegar. Test C: stir 1 spoon of sugar into about half a cup of warm water.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"Which of the three tests do you think will give you the most clues? Why?"
📊
Section 3
Recording What You See

For each of your three tests, you'll fill out a row of a table. You'll record three things: what you saw before you mixed, what you saw during the mix (any fizz? color? smell?), and what you saw after. You can also draw a small picture next to each row.

Real-World Connection: Chemists in real labs work like this all the time. They mix tiny amounts of two substances and look for clues. Sometimes a clue is a brand-new color. Sometimes it's heat. Sometimes it's bubbles. The clues guide their next test.

🔑 Key Concept

To test for a chemical change, mix two things and watch carefully. New bubbles, new color, new smell, or new feel are strong clues that a new substance just formed.

Mind-Blowing Fact
When vinegar and baking soda fizz, the new gas they make is the same gas you breathe out — carbon dioxide. That same fizz is what makes bread rise, what makes soda sparkle, and what helps plants grow.
Go Deeper — Why does the fizz stop after a while?

Once you mix vinegar and baking soda, you might notice the foam slows down and eventually stops. Why? Because chemical changes use up the starting materials. Either the vinegar runs out, or the baking soda does. When one of them is gone, there's nothing left to react with — so the fizz stops.

Real chemists call this a 'completed reaction.' You can sometimes restart the fizz by adding more of the missing ingredient — which is a clue that the reaction is real, not magic.

🔬 Three Mixing Tests
Two physical changes. One chemical change. Spot the clues.
⏱ 20 min total

Gather your materials. You'll need: a small clear cup or jar (a second one is helpful but not required), water (cool and warm), 2 teaspoons of salt, 2 teaspoons of sugar, 1 teaspoon of baking soda, and about ¼ cup of vinegar. Plus a spoon.

⚠️

Before you start: Test B (vinegar + baking soda) can foam up — do that one in the sink or over a paper towel. Don't lean your face over the cup. Wash hands after. Pour mixtures down the sink when finished. Never taste anything.

1

Test A — Salt in cool water

Pour about half a cup of cool tap water into a clear cup. Look closely at the salt before you add it — color, texture, smell. Add 1 teaspoon of salt and stir until you can't see the salt anymore. Write down what happened. Did anything bubble, change color, or smell different?

2

Test B — Vinegar + baking soda (over the sink!)

Set your second cup in the sink (or on a paper towel). Pour in about ¼ cup of vinegar. Smell carefully (don't inhale deeply). Now slowly sprinkle in 1 teaspoon of baking soda. Stand back! Watch what happens. Write down every clue you see, hear, or smell.

3

Test C — Sugar in warm water

In a clean cup, pour about half a cup of warm (not hot) tap water. Look at the sugar grains. Add 1 teaspoon of sugar and stir until clear. Write down what happened. Bubbles? New smell? New color?

4

Clean up & safety check

Pour all three mixtures down the sink. Rinse and dry your cups. Wash your hands with soap. Wipe up any drips on the counter.

Sketch what you saw: Draw each cup before and after mixing. Pay special attention to Test B — try to show the foam, bubbles, and overflowing fizz. A small picture next to each row of your table is great evidence.

5

Compare your observations

Compare your three rows. Which test had clear, dramatic clues? Which had almost no clues? In Test A and Test C, the cup looked clear and the salt or sugar seemed to "disappear" — but it didn't really. Where did it go? In Test B, what looked totally new?

6

Write a short claim

Write one sentence: "Out of my three tests, the one that made a new substance was ___ because ___." This will become part of your CER.

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In your lab, which test made a brand-new substance?
ATest A — salt in water
BTest B — vinegar plus baking soda
CTest C — sugar in warm water
✅ Correct! Vinegar + baking soda fizzes because a new gas (carbon dioxide) is being made. That's a chemical change. The other two tests just dissolved one ingredient into another — physical changes.
❌ Not quite. Which test had foam, fizz, and a new gas you could SEE? Only one test produced something brand new.
Which of these is the STRONGEST clue that a chemical change has happened?
AThe shape of the cup looks the same
BBubbles, foam, and a new gas appear out of nowhere
CThe temperature of the room stays the same
✅ Correct! Bubbles and a new gas appearing out of nowhere are strong evidence that the starting materials have reacted to form a new substance.
❌ Not quite. The cup's shape and the room's temperature aren't really clues about the substances inside. What new thing appeared during Test B?
Your friend mixes oil and water. The oil floats. She says, "A new substance just formed!" Was a chemical change happening?
AYes — anything that doesn't mix is a chemical change.
BYes — the oil layer is the new substance.
CNo — the oil and water are still the same substances; they just didn't blend. No new substance formed.
✅ Correct! Oil floating on water is a physical layering, not a chemical change. The oil is still oil. The water is still water. No new substance formed.
❌ Not quite. Just because things don't blend doesn't mean a new substance formed. Look for stronger clues — new color, new smell, gas, or a change you can't undo.
