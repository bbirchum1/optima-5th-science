📖 Plan Before You Test
A short setup before you grab the penny
⏱ 7 min
🪙
Section 1
Pick Your Variable

You'll test the same penny two different ways. The one thing that changes is the side facing up: heads or tails. That's your variablevariableThe one thing you change on purpose in an investigation..

Everything else has to stay exactly the same. Same penny. Same eyedropper or straw. Same way of releasing each drop. Same flat surface. Same water from the same cup. Those are your controlscontrolSomething kept the same in every trial. — and if you slip up on any of them, your test won't be fair.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Why does it have to be the same penny? What might change if you switched pennies halfway through?"
🔁
Section 2
Plan Your Trials

One drop count isn't enough. You'll run the test three times on each side — six trials total. Three for heads, three for tails. After each trial, you wipe the penny completely dry with a paper towel before you start the next one.

Why three? Because your first try might be unusually high or low — your hand might shake, or the penny might still have a tiny drop on it. The pattern across three trials is much more reliable than any single number.

🧪
Stop and Reason
"If your three heads-up trials were 32, 35, and 33 drops, what number would you use as your final answer? Why?"
🛡️
Section 3
Defend Your Answer

At the end, you're going to make a claim — heads holds more drops, or tails does, or there's no real difference. But a claim by itself isn't enough. You'll need to point to your data and explain why your test was fair.

Real-World Connection: Real scientists do this every day. When researchers test a new bridge material or a new medicine, they have to defend their results to other scientists. "Here's what I changed. Here's what I kept the same. Here's my data. Here's why my answer is trustworthy."

🔑 Key Concept

A trustworthy investigation has one variable, many controls, and repeated trials. The data you collect becomes the evidence that lets you defend your conclusion.

Mind-Blowing Fact
Water has a very strong "skin" called surface tension. That invisible skin is what lets a penny hold around 30 to 50 drops in a tiny dome before it finally breaks.
Go Deeper — Why does water dome up like that?

Water molecules at the surface pull each other inward more strongly than they pull on the air above them. That sideways pulling acts like a stretchy skin — and that skin can hold up surprising amounts of water before it gives way. Some insects use the same effect to walk across ponds without sinking.

If you add a single drop of dish soap to your cup, the surface tension breaks. Try it after you're done with your real trials — your drop count will collapse.

🔬 The Penny Drop Test
Six trials. Two sides. One conclusion you have to defend.
⏱ 25 min

Time to find out who's right — your prediction, or the data. Set up at a kitchen counter or table where it's okay if a little water spills. Have your paper towel ready.

Watch the water dome up — that's surface tension at work. Your penny can hold way more than you'd guess.
1

Set up your station

Place your penny on a flat surface, heads up. Put your cup of water next to it. Have your dropper (or straw) ready, plus a dry paper towel. Write today's question at the top of your notebook page: "Does a penny hold more drops heads-up or tails-up?"

2

Run Trial 1 — heads

Add one drop of water at a time. Count out loud as you go. Keep adding until the water finally spills over the edge of the penny. Record the LAST number that fit BEFORE the spill. Wipe the penny completely dry.

3

Repeat — twice more on heads, then three times on tails

Wipe the penny dry between every trial. After three heads-up trials, flip the penny and run three tails-up trials the same way. Record each number in the table below.

🧮

To find the average: add your three trials together, then divide by 3. Example: 32 + 35 + 33 = 100; 100 ÷ 3 ≈ 33 drops.

4

Compare your averages

Look at your two averages. Did heads hold more, did tails hold more, or were they very close? "Close" means within 2–3 drops — that probably isn't a real difference, just normal wobble between trials.

5

Make a claim you can defend

Write a one-sentence claim using your data: "Heads held more drops," "Tails held more drops," or "There was no real difference between the two sides." You'll use this in your CER.

Self-Check
Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you understand.
In the penny drop test, what was the variable?
Athe kind of water you used
Bwhich side of the penny faced up
Cthe number of drops you counted
✅ Correct! That's the one thing that changed on purpose — the variable. The water and dropper were controls; the number of drops was the data.
❌ Not quite. A variable is the one thing you change on purpose. The water type stayed the same. The drop count was what you measured.
Your three heads-up trials are 30 drops, 32 drops, and 31 drops. Your three tails-up trials are 28, 30, and 29. What can you conclude?
AThe two sides held about the same — the difference is too small to count as real.
BHeads is way better — by a huge amount.
CYou should ignore the data and trust your gut.
✅ Correct! Averages of about 31 and 29 are basically the same. A 2-drop difference is probably just trial-to-trial wobble, not a real pattern.
❌ Not quite. Compare the averages — 31 vs. 29. That's a tiny gap. With normal trial wobble, you'd need a bigger difference to call it a real pattern.
You finish the test and your friend says, "Your answer doesn't count. You only ran three trials per side." How would you defend your work?
A"You're right, my answer is meaningless."
B"It doesn't matter how many trials you do."
C"Three trials give us a pattern. If the three numbers are close, the average is reliable. More trials would make it even stronger, but three is enough to see the pattern."
✅ Correct! That's exactly how scientists defend their work — point to the pattern in your trials, not just one number. More trials would help, but three is a real start.
❌ Not quite. Three trials is a real start — what matters is that your numbers are close. A scientist would point to the pattern, not give up on the test.
